Biostatistics in Practice

Survival Analysis

“The question is not whether patients die, but when — and whether we can delay that event.” — David Cox (paraphrased)

The Problem

You are analyzing the TCGA breast cancer cohort. A clinical collaborator asks: “Does TP53 mutation status affect overall survival? If so, by how much? And does the effect hold after adjusting for age, stage, and ER status?”

This seems like a simple comparison — take TP53-mutant and TP53-wild-type patients, compare their survival times. But survival data has a fundamental complication that makes standard tests (t-tests, linear regression) invalid: censoring.

Some patients are alive at the end of the study. Some dropped out. Some died of unrelated causes. For all of these, you know they survived at least as long as their recorded time, but you do not know their actual survival time. Throwing away censored observations wastes data. Treating their last-known-alive time as their survival time underestimates true survival. You need methods designed specifically for this data structure.

This chapter covers the essential toolkit: Kaplan-Meier curves for visualization, the log-rank test for comparing groups, and Cox proportional hazards regression for adjusting for covariates.

Why It Matters

Censoring Is the Rule, Not the Exception

In any prospective clinical study, censoring is unavoidable:

  • Administrative censoring: The study ends before all patients have died. In a 5-year study of breast cancer with 85% 5-year survival, most patients are censored.
  • Loss to follow-up: Patients move, change doctors, or withdraw consent.
  • Competing risks: A patient with breast cancer dies in a car accident. Their breast cancer survival is censored.

In TCGA BRCA, approximately 85% of patients are censored (alive at last follow-up). If you analyze only the 15% who died, you lose 85% of your data and introduce massive selection bias.

Survival Analysis Powers Clinical Trials

Every phase III cancer clinical trial uses survival analysis as the primary endpoint. Regulatory approvals (FDA, EMA) for cancer drugs require demonstration of improved overall survival or progression-free survival. The methods in this chapter — KM curves, log-rank test, Cox regression, hazard ratios — are the language of oncology clinical trials.

Genomic Biomarkers Need Survival Context

A gene mutation that does not affect survival is clinically less actionable than one that does. TP53 mutations are found in ~30% of breast cancers and are associated with worse prognosis — but how much worse, and does this hold across all subtypes? Only survival analysis can answer this.

The Analysis

Part 1: Kaplan-Meier Survival Curves

The Kaplan-Meier (KM) estimator is the standard non-parametric method for estimating the survival function from censored data.

How it works:

At each event time t_i:

  • n_i = number of patients at risk (still alive and not censored before t_i)
  • d_i = number of deaths at t_i
  • Conditional survival probability at t_i: (n_i - d_i) / n_i
  • Cumulative survival: product of all conditional probabilities up to t

S(t) = product over all t_i <= t of (1 - d_i / n_i)

The result is a step function that decreases at each event time and is unchanged during censored intervals.

# Kaplan-Meier curve for all patients
let km_all = kaplan_meier(time_clean, status_clean)
println("Overall Kaplan-Meier estimate:")
println("  1-year survival: " + to_string(round(km_all.survival_at(365.0) * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  3-year survival: " + to_string(round(km_all.survival_at(1095.0) * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  5-year survival: " + to_string(round(km_all.survival_at(1825.0) * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  10-year survival: " + to_string(round(km_all.survival_at(3650.0) * 100.0, 1)) + "%")

if !is_none(km_all.median_survival) then
    println("  Median survival: " + to_string(round(km_all.median_survival / 365.25, 2)) + " years")
else
    println("  Median survival: Not reached (>50% still alive)")
end

Median Survival Time

The median survival is the time at which the KM estimate crosses 0.5 (50%). If more than half the patients are still alive at the end of follow-up, the median is “not reached” — you cannot estimate it. This is common in early-stage cancers with good prognosis.

Part 2: The Log-Rank Test

The log-rank test compares survival distributions between two or more groups. It is the standard test for KM curves.

How it works:

At each event time, the test compares the observed number of events in each group to the expected number under the null hypothesis (no difference between groups). The test statistic is the sum of these differences across all event times.

Under the null hypothesis, the test statistic follows a chi-squared distribution with k-1 degrees of freedom (k = number of groups).

# Log-rank test: TP53-mutant vs wild-type
# We need to combine the data with group labels

let all_times = concat(time_mut_days, time_wt_days)
let all_status = concat(status_mut, status_wt)
let all_groups = concat(
    time_mut_days |> map(|_| 1.0),  # 1 = TP53-mutant
    time_wt_days |> map(|_| 0.0)    # 0 = TP53-wild-type
)

# The kaplan_meier function with two groups performs the log-rank test
let km_comparison = kaplan_meier(all_times, all_status, all_groups)

println("Log-Rank Test: TP53-mutant vs TP53-wild-type")
println("  Chi-squared statistic: " + to_string(round(km_comparison.log_rank_chi2, 4)))
println("  Degrees of freedom: 1")
println("  P-value: " + to_string(km_comparison.log_rank_p))

if km_comparison.log_rank_p < 0.05 then
    println("  Result: Significant difference in survival (p < 0.05)")
else
    println("  Result: No significant difference in survival (p >= 0.05)")
end

The log-rank test tells you whether the survival curves differ. It does not tell you by how much. For that, we need hazard ratios.

Part 3: Hazard Ratios

The hazard at time t is the instantaneous rate of the event (death) occurring at time t, given survival up to t. It is like a conditional probability per unit time.

The hazard ratio (HR) compares the hazard in one group to the hazard in another:

HR = hazard(TP53-mutant) / hazard(TP53-wild-type)

  • HR = 1: No difference in hazard
  • HR = 2: TP53-mutant patients have double the death rate at any given time
  • HR = 0.5: TP53-mutant patients have half the death rate

Hazard ratios are to survival analysis what odds ratios are to logistic regression: the natural effect size measure.

Part 4: Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox PH model estimates hazard ratios while adjusting for covariates.

The model:

h(t) = h_0(t) * exp(beta_1X_1 + beta_2X_2 + … + beta_p*X_p)

  • h(t): hazard at time t
  • h_0(t): baseline hazard (left unspecified — this is what makes it “semi-parametric”)
  • exp(beta_i): hazard ratio for a one-unit increase in X_i

The beauty of the Cox model is that the baseline hazard h_0(t) cancels out when comparing groups, so you do not need to specify it. You only estimate the betas.

# Univariate Cox model: survival ~ TP53 mutation
let tp53_status = col_values(surv_data, "tp53_mutant") |> map(|x| to_float(x))
let surv_times = col_values(surv_data, "OS.time") |> map(|x| to_float(x))
let surv_events = col_values(surv_data, "OS") |> map(|x| to_float(x))

let cox_tp53 = cox_ph(surv_times, surv_events, tp53_status)

println("Cox Proportional Hazards: Survival ~ TP53 Mutation")
println("  TP53 coefficient (beta): " + to_string(round(cox_tp53.coefficients[0], 4)))
println("  Hazard Ratio (exp(beta)): " + to_string(round(exp(cox_tp53.coefficients[0]), 4)))
println("  95% CI: (" + to_string(round(exp(cox_tp53.ci_lower[0]), 4))
    + ", " + to_string(round(exp(cox_tp53.ci_upper[0]), 4)) + ")")
println("  P-value: " + to_string(cox_tp53.p_values[0]))
println("  Concordance: " + to_string(round(cox_tp53.concordance, 4)))

Interpreting the Hazard Ratio

If the HR for TP53 mutation is 2.1 with 95% CI (1.5, 2.9), this means:

  • TP53-mutant patients have 2.1 times the death rate compared to wild-type, at any given time point during the study
  • We are 95% confident the true HR is between 1.5 and 2.9
  • The CI excludes 1.0, so the effect is statistically significant

What HR does NOT mean:

  • TP53-mutant patients survive half as long (HR is a rate ratio, not a time ratio)
  • 2.1 times as many TP53-mutant patients die (HR is an instantaneous rate, not a cumulative count)
  • TP53 mutation causes death (observational study, potential confounders)
# The concordance index (C-index) measures discrimination
# C = 0.5: random predictions
# C = 0.7: acceptable
# C = 0.8: excellent
# C = 1.0: perfect

println("Model discrimination:")
println("  Concordance (C-index): " + to_string(round(cox_tp53.concordance, 4)))
println("  Interpretation: The model correctly ranks " +
    to_string(round(cox_tp53.concordance * 100.0, 1)) +
    "% of patient pairs by survival time")

Part 5: Multivariable Cox Regression

The univariate result may be confounded. TP53 mutations are more common in ER-negative breast cancer, which itself has worse prognosis. We need to adjust for potential confounders.

# Prepare covariates
let age = col_values(surv_data, "age_at_initial_pathologic_diagnosis")
    |> map(|x| if is_none(x) then 60.0 else to_float(x))

let stage_numeric = col_values(surv_data, "pathologic_stage") |> map(|s|
    let s_str = to_string(s)
    if contains(s_str, "IV") then 4.0
    else if contains(s_str, "III") then 3.0
    else if contains(s_str, "II") then 2.0
    else if contains(s_str, "I") then 1.0
    else 2.0  # impute with median stage for missing
)

let er_numeric = col_values(surv_data, "breast_carcinoma_estrogen_receptor_status")
    |> map(|s|
        if to_string(s) == "Positive" then 1.0 else 0.0
    )

# Multivariable Cox model
let pred_matrix = matrix([tp53_status, age, stage_numeric, er_numeric])
let cox_multi = cox_ph(surv_times, surv_events, pred_matrix)

println("Multivariable Cox Model:")
println("  Survival ~ TP53 + Age + Stage + ER Status")
println("")
let var_names = ["TP53 mutation", "Age (per year)", "Stage (per level)", "ER positive"]
println("  Variable          HR      95% CI              p-value")
println("  ────────          ──      ──────              ───────")
zip(var_names, cox_multi.coefficients) |> each(|pair|
    let idx = var_names |> map(|v| v == pair[0])
    let hr = exp(pair[1])
    println("  " + pair[0] + "    " + to_string(round(hr, 3)))
)

# Detailed output with confidence intervals
let i = 0
var_names |> each(|name|
    let hr = exp(cox_multi.coefficients[i])
    let ci_lo = exp(cox_multi.ci_lower[i])
    let ci_hi = exp(cox_multi.ci_upper[i])
    let p = cox_multi.p_values[i]
    println("  " + name + ":")
    println("    HR = " + to_string(round(hr, 3))
        + " (95% CI: " + to_string(round(ci_lo, 3))
        + " - " + to_string(round(ci_hi, 3))
        + "), p = " + to_string(round(p, 4)))
    i = i + 1
)

Interpreting the Adjusted Model

Compare the adjusted and unadjusted hazard ratios for TP53:

# Compare univariate vs multivariable HR for TP53
let hr_unadj = exp(cox_tp53.coefficients[0])
let hr_adj = exp(cox_multi.coefficients[0])

println("TP53 Hazard Ratio:")
println("  Unadjusted: " + to_string(round(hr_unadj, 3)))
println("  Adjusted (for age, stage, ER): " + to_string(round(hr_adj, 3)))

let pct_change = abs(hr_adj - hr_unadj) / hr_unadj * 100.0
println("  Change: " + to_string(round(pct_change, 1)) + "%")

if pct_change > 10.0 then
    println("  Substantial confounding detected (>10% change)")
    println("  The adjusted HR is the more valid estimate")
else
    println("  Minimal confounding (<10% change)")
    println("  Unadjusted and adjusted estimates are similar")
end

If the adjusted HR is substantially smaller than the unadjusted HR, part of the TP53 effect was mediated through its correlation with ER status and stage. The adjusted HR represents the independent prognostic value of TP53 mutations.

Part 6: Forest Plot of Hazard Ratios

The forest plot is the standard visualization for Cox regression results. Each covariate gets a row with its HR and 95% CI displayed graphically.

# Generate forest plot data
let forest_data = table(
    ["variable", "hr", "ci_lower", "ci_upper", "p_value"],
    [
        var_names,
        cox_multi.coefficients |> map(|b| exp(b)),
        cox_multi.ci_lower |> map(|b| exp(b)),
        cox_multi.ci_upper |> map(|b| exp(b)),
        cox_multi.p_values
    ]
)

println("Forest plot data:")
println(forest_data)

forest_plot(forest_data, "Multivariable Cox Model — Hazard Ratios")
    |> save_plot("cox_forest_plot.svg")

The forest plot shows:

  • A vertical line at HR = 1.0 (null effect)
  • For each variable, a point (HR) with horizontal lines (95% CI)
  • If the CI crosses 1.0, the effect is not significant
  • Effects to the right of 1.0 increase hazard (worse prognosis)
  • Effects to the left of 1.0 decrease hazard (better prognosis)

Part 7: Checking the Proportional Hazards Assumption

The Cox model assumes that hazard ratios are constant over time. This is the “proportional hazards” assumption. If the HR changes over time, the model is misspecified.

How to check:

  1. Graphical: Log-log KM plots. If the log(-log(S(t))) curves are parallel, the PH assumption holds.
  2. Statistical: Schoenfeld residuals. Test whether the residuals correlate with time.
# Check PH assumption graphically
# Log-log survival plot: log(-log(S(t))) vs log(t)
# Parallel curves = PH assumption satisfied

let loglog_mut = km_mut.survival_probs
    |> filter(|s| s > 0.0 && s < 1.0)
    |> map(|s| log(-log(s)))
let loglog_wt = km_wt.survival_probs
    |> filter(|s| s > 0.0 && s < 1.0)
    |> map(|s| log(-log(s)))

let log_times_mut = km_mut.times
    |> slice(0, len(loglog_mut))
    |> map(|t| log(t))
let log_times_wt = km_wt.times
    |> slice(0, len(loglog_wt))
    |> map(|t| log(t))

scatter(log_times_mut, loglog_mut, "Log-log Plot: TP53-mutant")
    |> save_plot("loglog_tp53_mut.svg")
scatter(log_times_wt, loglog_wt, "Log-log Plot: TP53-wild-type")
    |> save_plot("loglog_tp53_wt.svg")

println("Proportional hazards check:")
println("  If the log-log curves are roughly parallel,")
println("  the PH assumption is reasonable.")
println("  Crossing or diverging curves indicate violation.")

Part 9: Time-Dependent Effects

Sometimes the hazard ratio is not constant over time. For example, the effect of a treatment may be strongest in the first year and diminish afterwards. Or the effect of a mutation may only become apparent after several years.

# Split analysis by time period to check for time-dependent effects
# Early (0-3 years) vs Late (3+ years)

let early_cutoff = 3.0 * 365.25  # 3 years in days

# Early events: censor everyone at 3 years
let early_time = surv_times |> map(|t| min(t, early_cutoff))
let early_status = zip(surv_times, surv_events) |> map(|pair|
    if pair[0] <= early_cutoff then pair[1]
    else 0.0  # censor at cutoff
)

let cox_early = cox_ph(early_time, early_status, tp53_status)
let hr_early = exp(cox_early.coefficients[0])

# Late events: only patients surviving past 3 years
let late_idx = surv_times |> map(|t| t > early_cutoff)
let late_time = zip(surv_times, late_idx)
    |> filter(|pair| pair[1])
    |> map(|pair| pair[0] - early_cutoff)
let late_status = zip(surv_events, late_idx)
    |> filter(|pair| pair[1])
    |> map(|pair| pair[0])
let late_tp53 = zip(tp53_status, late_idx)
    |> filter(|pair| pair[1])
    |> map(|pair| pair[0])

let cox_late = cox_ph(late_time, late_status, late_tp53)
let hr_late = exp(cox_late.coefficients[0])

println("Time-Dependent Effect of TP53:")
println("  0-3 years HR: " + to_string(round(hr_early, 3))
    + " (p = " + to_string(round(cox_early.p_values[0], 4)) + ")")
println("  3+ years HR:  " + to_string(round(hr_late, 3))
    + " (p = " + to_string(round(cox_late.p_values[0], 4)) + ")")

if abs(hr_early - hr_late) > 0.5 then
    println("  Substantial difference — PH assumption may be violated")
    println("  The TP53 effect may change over time")
else
    println("  Similar HRs across periods — PH assumption reasonable")
end

Interpretation

What We Found

  1. TP53 mutations are associated with worse overall survival in TCGA BRCA, with a hazard ratio around 2.0.

  2. The effect is partially confounded by ER status and stage. The adjusted HR is somewhat smaller than the unadjusted HR, indicating that TP53 mutations are prognostic partly because they mark more aggressive tumor subtypes.

  3. The adjusted effect remains significant, suggesting TP53 mutations have independent prognostic value beyond subtype classification.

  4. Multiple genes affect survival, but after correction for multiple testing, only a few remain significant.

What We Cannot Conclude

  • TP53 mutations do not “cause” death. They are markers of a more aggressive tumor biology. Restoring p53 function may or may not improve survival.

  • The HR is an average over time. If the proportional hazards assumption is violated, the HR summarizes a changing effect as a single number.

  • The TCGA cohort is not a clinical trial. It is an observational study with heterogeneous treatment. Survival differences may reflect treatment differences (TP53-mutant patients may have received different treatment because of their subtype).

Median Survival vs Hazard Ratio

These are different quantities:

  • Median survival is an absolute measure: “Half the patients survive beyond X years”
  • Hazard ratio is a relative measure: “Group A has Y times the death rate of Group B”

A disease with median survival of 20 years and HR of 2.0 for a mutation means the mutation reduces median survival to approximately 10 years (rough approximation). A disease with median survival of 6 months and HR of 2.0 means the mutation reduces median survival to approximately 3 months.

Same HR, very different clinical impact. Always report both the absolute survival estimates and the relative hazard ratios.

Decision

When to Use Each Method

QuestionMethod
What is the survival probability at time t?Kaplan-Meier
Do two groups have different survival?Log-rank test
What is the relative hazard between groups?Cox PH (univariate)
What is the adjusted relative hazard?Cox PH (multivariable)
Does the hazard change over time?Time-dependent Cox or restricted mean survival time

Reporting Survival Analysis Results

A complete survival analysis report includes:

  1. Sample size and events: N patients, N events, median follow-up time
  2. KM estimates: Survival probability at clinically meaningful time points (1-year, 5-year, etc.)
  3. Median survival with 95% CI (or “not reached” if >50% survived)
  4. Log-rank p-value: For group comparisons
  5. Hazard ratios with 95% CI: From Cox regression
  6. Adjusted analysis: If confounders are present
  7. PH assumption check: Report whether the assumption was verified

Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Treating Censored Observations as Events

# This is the most fundamental error in survival analysis
println("WRONG: Excluding censored patients")
println("  Throws away ~85% of the data in TCGA BRCA")
println("  Biases toward patients who die early")
println("")
println("WRONG: Treating last follow-up as death time")
println("  Underestimates true survival")
println("  Makes every patient appear to eventually die")
println("")
println("RIGHT: Use Kaplan-Meier or Cox regression")
println("  Properly handles censored observations")
println("  Censored patients contribute information up to their censoring time")

Pitfall 2: Comparing Survival Times with T-Tests

# A t-test comparing mean survival time between groups is wrong
# because censored values are not real survival times

let bad_ttest = ttest(time_mut, time_wt)
println("T-test comparing survival times (WRONG):")
println("  p = " + to_string(round(bad_ttest.p_value, 4)))
println("")
println("Problems with this approach:")
println("  1. Censored observations are treated as exact survival times")
println("  2. Survival times are right-skewed (non-normal)")
println("  3. Result is biased toward the direction of longer follow-up")
println("")
println("Use log-rank test instead:")
println("  p = " + to_string(km_comparison.log_rank_p))

Pitfall 3: Immortal Time Bias

Immortal time bias occurs when the exposure classification requires surviving long enough to receive it. For example, if you classify patients as “received adjuvant chemotherapy” vs “did not,” the chemotherapy group had to survive long enough to start treatment — creating an artificial survival advantage.

println("Immortal time bias example:")
println("  Study: Does adjuvant chemo improve survival?")
println("  Patients who die before chemo could start are classified")
println("  as 'no chemo' — guaranteed short survival in the no-chemo group")
println("")
println("Solutions:")
println("  1. Landmark analysis: Only include patients alive at a fixed time")
println("     point (e.g., 90 days post-surgery)")
println("  2. Time-dependent covariates: Treatment status changes over time")
println("  3. Start time from treatment, not diagnosis")

Pitfall 4: Guarantee-Time Bias in Genomics

A genomic version of immortal time bias: if you define groups based on molecular features that require tissue from surgery, patients who die before surgery are excluded, biasing the entire cohort toward longer survival.

println("Guarantee-time bias in genomics:")
println("  TCGA patients all had surgery (required for tissue)")
println("  Patients who died before surgery are not in the cohort")
println("  This means the cohort is healthier than the general")
println("  cancer population — survival estimates are too optimistic")

Pitfall 5: Multiple Testing in Multi-Gene Analysis

# Testing 10 genes (or worse, 20,000) without correction
println("Multi-gene survival testing:")
println("  Testing " + to_string(len(genes_to_test)) + " genes at alpha = 0.05")
println("  Expected false positives by chance: "
    + to_string(round(to_float(len(genes_to_test)) * 0.05, 1)))
println("")
println("  ALWAYS apply multiple testing correction (BH method)")
println("  Report both raw and adjusted p-values")
println("  Use adjusted p-values for significance decisions")

Pitfall 6: Overfitting with Many Covariates

# Rule of thumb: need ~10-15 events per covariate in Cox regression
let events_per_covariate = to_float(n_events) / 4.0  # 4 covariates in our model
println("Events per covariate: " + to_string(round(events_per_covariate, 1)))

if events_per_covariate < 10.0 then
    println("WARNING: Fewer than 10 events per covariate")
    println("  Model may be overfitted")
    println("  Reduce the number of covariates or increase sample size")
else
    println("OK: Sufficient events per covariate (>= 10)")
end

Where Next?

Immediate Extensions

  • Competing risks: When patients can experience multiple types of events (death from cancer vs death from other causes). Standard KM overestimates the incidence of each event type because it treats competing events as censoring.

  • Restricted mean survival time (RMST): An alternative to hazard ratios that does not require the proportional hazards assumption. RMST is the area under the KM curve up to a specified time point.

  • Subgroup analysis: Does the TP53 effect differ by subtype? Run separate Cox models within ER+ and ER- subgroups, or include an interaction term (TP53 * ER status).

Advanced Topics

  • Frailty models: Random effects in survival analysis. Account for unobserved heterogeneity (e.g., unmeasured genetic background).

  • Joint modeling: Link longitudinal biomarker measurements (e.g., PSA trajectory) with time-to-event outcomes. The longitudinal model informs the survival model in real time.

  • Multi-state models: Patients can transition through multiple states (disease-free → recurrence → death). More realistic than simple alive/dead models.

  3. Multi-gene Cox: Build a multivariable Cox model with TP53, PIK3CA, GATA3, and CDH1 mutation status (all simultaneously). Which genes are independently prognostic?

  4. PH assumption: For each gene in problem 3, check the proportional hazards assumption using log-log plots. Does any gene violate the assumption?

  5. Landmark analysis: Restrict the cohort to patients alive at 1 year post-diagnosis. How does this change the TP53 hazard ratio? Why?

Key takeaways from this chapter:

  • Survival data has censoring — you must use methods designed for it (KM, log-rank, Cox), not t-tests or linear regression.
  • Kaplan-Meier curves estimate survival probability non-parametrically.
  • The log-rank test compares survival between groups.
  • Cox regression provides hazard ratios adjusted for confounders.
  • Hazard ratios are relative measures — always report absolute survival estimates (KM) alongside them.
  • Check the proportional hazards assumption before trusting Cox results.
  • Apply multiple testing correction when scanning many genes.
  • Be aware of immortal time bias, guarantee-time bias, and the difference between observational and trial data.
