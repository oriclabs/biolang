Biostatistics in Practice

Batch Effects & Confounders

“The first rule of bioinformatics: 90% of the signal in your data is technical variation. The biology is in the other 10%.” — Anonymous

The Problem

Two labs contribute RNA-seq data to a collaborative breast cancer study. Lab A processed 50 samples at their sequencing center in January. Lab B processed 50 samples at a different center in March. When you run PCA on the combined expression matrix, the first principal component separates the two labs perfectly — not the tumor subtypes, not the treatment groups, the labs.

The biology you care about (differential expression between tumor subtypes) is buried under a mountain of technical variation. If you ignore the batch effect, you will report hundreds of “differentially expressed” genes that are actually just genes affected by the different library preparation protocols, sequencing depths, or even the ambient temperature in the two sequencing rooms.

This chapter teaches you to detect, quantify, and handle batch effects — and introduces the broader concept of confounding, which is the single most important threat to valid inference in observational studies.

Why It Matters

Batch Effects Are Universal

Every high-throughput technology introduces batch effects:

  • RNA-seq: Library preparation, sequencing lane, flow cell, sequencing center, RNA extraction method, time between extraction and sequencing
  • Microarrays: Chip lot, hybridization date, scanner calibration
  • Mass spectrometry: Instrument calibration drift, column age, ionization efficiency
  • Single-cell RNA-seq: Capture efficiency, ambient RNA contamination, cell viability differences between runs
  • Whole genome sequencing: Coverage depth, PCR duplicate rate, alignment software version

In a landmark 2010 paper, Leek et al. showed that batch effects are present in the vast majority of published genomic studies and that they frequently lead to incorrect conclusions.

The Cost of Ignoring Batch Effects

  • False discoveries: Genes that differ between batches are reported as biologically significant
  • Failed replication: Results from one batch do not replicate in another
  • Incorrect clustering: PCA and clustering algorithms group samples by batch, not biology
  • Wasted resources: Follow-up experiments on batch-effect genes waste time and money
  • Retracted papers: Several high-profile genomics papers have been retracted or corrected because batch effects were not properly handled

Confounding Is Even More Dangerous

Batch effects are a special case of confounding — when a technical or demographic variable is correlated with both the predictor and the outcome.

If Lab A processed mostly ER-positive tumors and Lab B processed mostly ER-negative tumors, you cannot tell whether gene expression differences are due to ER status or lab. The batch is confounded with the biology.

This is the most dangerous scenario because no statistical correction can fully separate the two effects. Confounding must be prevented at the design stage, not fixed in the analysis.

Exploration

What Do Batch Effects Look Like?

Batch effects manifest as systematic differences in measured values between groups of samples processed differently. They can affect:

  1. Mean expression level: All genes are shifted up or down (library size effect)
  2. Variance: Some batches have more variable measurements
  3. Specific genes: Certain genes are affected more than others (GC content bias, gene length bias)
  4. Correlation structure: Gene-gene correlations change between batches
# Simulate a dataset with batch effects to demonstrate the principles
# 20 samples from each of 2 batches, measuring 1000 genes
# True biology: 2 groups (treatment/control), 10 samples each per batch

let n_genes = 1000
let n_samples = 40

# Generate true expression (same in both batches)
let true_expr = []
let g = 0
let gene_means = rnorm(n_genes, 8.0, 3.0)  # gene-level mean expression

# Batch effect: shift all genes by a constant
let batch_shift = 2.0  # log2 units

# Generate expression matrix
# Samples 1-20: Batch A, Samples 21-40: Batch B
# Samples 1-10 and 21-30: Control, Samples 11-20 and 31-40: Treatment

let batch_labels = concat(
    rnorm(20, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Batch_A"),
    rnorm(20, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Batch_B")
)
let group_labels = concat(
    concat(
        rnorm(10, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Control"),
        rnorm(10, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Treatment")
    ),
    concat(
        rnorm(10, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Control"),
        rnorm(10, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Treatment")
    )
)

println("Experimental design:")
println("  Batch A: 10 control + 10 treatment = 20 samples")
println("  Batch B: 10 control + 10 treatment = 20 samples")
println("  (Balanced design — batch is NOT confounded with group)")

# Simulate expression with batch effect
# For each gene, Batch B has expression shifted by batch_shift

let sim_data = gene_means |> map(|mu|
    let batch_a = rnorm(20, mu, 1.0)
    let batch_b = rnorm(20, mu + batch_shift, 1.0)
    concat(batch_a, batch_b)
)

let sim_matrix = matrix(sim_data)

println("Simulated expression matrix: "
    + to_string(len(gene_means)) + " genes x "
    + to_string(n_samples) + " samples")

# Overall mean by batch
let batch_a_means = sim_data |> map(|gene| mean(slice(gene, 0, 20)))
let batch_b_means = sim_data |> map(|gene| mean(slice(gene, 20, 40)))

println("Mean expression per gene:")
println("  Batch A: " + to_string(round(mean(batch_a_means), 2)))
println("  Batch B: " + to_string(round(mean(batch_b_means), 2)))
println("  Difference: " + to_string(round(mean(batch_b_means) - mean(batch_a_means), 2)))
println("  (True batch effect was " + to_string(batch_shift) + ")")

Detecting Batch Effects with PCA

PCA is the most common method for detecting batch effects. If the first principal components separate samples by batch rather than by biology, you have a batch effect problem.

# PCA on simulated data
let pca_result = pca(sim_matrix)

println("PCA of simulated data:")
println("  PC1 variance explained: " + to_string(round(pca_result.variance_explained[0] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  PC2 variance explained: " + to_string(round(pca_result.variance_explained[1] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")

# Plot PCA colored by batch
pca_plot(pca_result, batch_labels, "PCA Colored by Batch (Before Correction)")
    |> save_plot("pca_batch_before.svg")

# Plot PCA colored by biological group
pca_plot(pca_result, group_labels, "PCA Colored by Treatment Group (Before Correction)")
    |> save_plot("pca_group_before.svg")

In the batch-colored plot, you will see two distinct clusters on PC1 — Batch A on one side, Batch B on the other. In the group-colored plot, the treatment and control groups are mixed within each batch cluster. PC1 captures the batch effect, not the biology.

# Quantify the batch effect using PC1 scores
let pc1_scores = pca_result.scores |> map(|row| row[0])

let pc1_batch_a = slice(pc1_scores, 0, 20)
let pc1_batch_b = slice(pc1_scores, 20, 40)

let pc1_test = ttest(pc1_batch_a, pc1_batch_b)
println("PC1 vs Batch:")
println("  Batch A PC1 mean: " + to_string(round(mean(pc1_batch_a), 2)))
println("  Batch B PC1 mean: " + to_string(round(mean(pc1_batch_b), 2)))
println("  t-test p-value: " + to_string(pc1_test.p_value))

# Compare with PC1 vs treatment group
let pc1_control = concat(slice(pc1_scores, 0, 10), slice(pc1_scores, 20, 30))
let pc1_treatment = concat(slice(pc1_scores, 10, 20), slice(pc1_scores, 30, 40))

let pc1_group_test = ttest(pc1_control, pc1_treatment)
println("PC1 vs Treatment:")
println("  Control PC1 mean: " + to_string(round(mean(pc1_control), 2)))
println("  Treatment PC1 mean: " + to_string(round(mean(pc1_treatment), 2)))
println("  t-test p-value: " + to_string(pc1_group_test.p_value))

If PC1 is significantly associated with batch but not with treatment, the batch effect dominates the biology. This is the classic batch effect signature.

The Analysis

Part 1: Library Size as a Confounder

The most basic batch effect in RNA-seq is library size — the total number of reads per sample. Samples with more reads have higher counts for every gene.

# Simulate library size variation
let lib_sizes_a = rnorm(20, 30.0, 5.0)   # millions of reads, Batch A
let lib_sizes_b = rnorm(20, 50.0, 5.0)   # millions of reads, Batch B (deeper)
let lib_sizes = concat(lib_sizes_a, lib_sizes_b)

println("Library sizes (millions of reads):")
println("  Batch A mean: " + to_string(round(mean(lib_sizes_a), 1)))
println("  Batch B mean: " + to_string(round(mean(lib_sizes_b), 1)))
println("  Ratio: " + to_string(round(mean(lib_sizes_b) / mean(lib_sizes_a), 2)) + "x")

# Before normalization: raw counts correlate with library size
# This creates a spurious positive correlation between ALL genes
println("")
println("Before normalization:")
println("  Every gene appears 'upregulated' in Batch B")
println("  Correlation between any two genes is inflated")
println("  PCA separates batches perfectly")

# Library size normalization methods

# 1. CPM (Counts Per Million) — simplest
# CPM = count / total_counts * 1,000,000

let raw_counts = rnorm(40, 500.0, 200.0) |> map(|x| abs(x))
let total_counts = lib_sizes |> map(|x| x * 1000000.0)
let cpm_vals = zip(raw_counts, total_counts) |> map(|pair| pair[0] / pair[1] * 1000000.0)

println("CPM normalization:")
println("  Divides each count by library size")
println("  Makes samples comparable in total counts")
println("  Does NOT account for compositional effects")

# 2. TPM (Transcripts Per Million) — accounts for gene length
println("")
println("TPM normalization:")
println("  Divides by gene length first (RPKM), then normalizes to sum to 1M")
println("  Makes samples comparable AND accounts for gene length")
println("  Better than FPKM for between-sample comparison")

# 3. DESeq2 size factors — median of ratios
println("")
println("DESeq2 normalization:")
println("  Uses median of ratios to a reference sample")
println("  Robust to outlier genes (e.g., one highly expressed gene)")
println("  Standard for differential expression analysis")

# Demonstrate CPM normalization on our simulated data
# Apply CPM to remove library size effect

let normalized_data = sim_data |> map(|gene|
    zip(gene, lib_sizes) |> map(|pair|
        pair[0] / pair[1] * 1.0  # simplified normalization
    )
)

# Re-run PCA after normalization
let norm_matrix = matrix(normalized_data)
let pca_norm = pca(norm_matrix)

println("PCA after library size normalization:")
println("  PC1 variance explained: " + to_string(round(pca_norm.variance_explained[0] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  PC2 variance explained: " + to_string(round(pca_norm.variance_explained[1] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")

pca_plot(pca_norm, batch_labels, "PCA After Normalization (Colored by Batch)")
    |> save_plot("pca_batch_normalized.svg")

Library size normalization helps but may not remove all batch effects. Deeper batch effects (GC content bias, RNA degradation differences, different kit versions) require more sophisticated correction.

Part 2: ComBat-Style Batch Correction

ComBat (from the sva R package) is the most widely used batch correction method. It uses an empirical Bayes framework to shrink batch effect estimates toward a common value, which is more robust than gene-by-gene correction.

The principle:

For each gene, model expression as:

Y_ij = alpha + Xbeta + batch_jgamma + delta_j*epsilon

Where:

  • alpha: overall mean
  • X*beta: biological effects (treatment, etc.)
  • gamma: additive batch effect (shift)
  • delta: multiplicative batch effect (scaling)
  • epsilon: residual

ComBat estimates gamma and delta for each gene, applies empirical Bayes shrinkage, then subtracts the batch effects from the data.

# Regression-based batch correction (simplified ComBat)
# For each gene, regress out the batch effect

let corrected_data = sim_data |> map(|gene|
    # Fit: expression ~ batch
    let model = lm(gene, batch_numeric)
    let batch_effect = model.coefficients[0]

    # Subtract batch effect from Batch B samples
    gene |> map(|val|
        val  # keep Batch A as-is
    )
    # More precisely: subtract fitted batch component
    zip(gene, batch_numeric) |> map(|pair|
        pair[0] - batch_effect * pair[1]
    )
)

let corrected_matrix = matrix(corrected_data)
let pca_corrected = pca(corrected_matrix)

println("PCA after regression-based batch correction:")
println("  PC1 variance explained: " + to_string(round(pca_corrected.variance_explained[0] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  PC2 variance explained: " + to_string(round(pca_corrected.variance_explained[1] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")

pca_plot(pca_corrected, batch_labels, "PCA After Batch Correction (Colored by Batch)")
    |> save_plot("pca_batch_corrected.svg")

pca_plot(pca_corrected, group_labels, "PCA After Batch Correction (Colored by Group)")
    |> save_plot("pca_group_corrected.svg")

After correction, the PCA should no longer separate samples by batch. The biological signal (treatment vs control) should now be more visible.

# Verify batch effect removal
let pc1_corrected = pca_corrected.scores |> map(|row| row[0])

let pc1_corr_a = slice(pc1_corrected, 0, 20)
let pc1_corr_b = slice(pc1_corrected, 20, 40)

let batch_test_after = ttest(pc1_corr_a, pc1_corr_b)
println("Batch effect in PC1 after correction:")
println("  Batch A mean: " + to_string(round(mean(pc1_corr_a), 4)))
println("  Batch B mean: " + to_string(round(mean(pc1_corr_b), 4)))
println("  p-value: " + to_string(round(batch_test_after.p_value, 4)))
println("  (Should be non-significant if correction worked)")

# Check that biological signal is preserved
let pc1_corr_ctrl = concat(slice(pc1_corrected, 0, 10), slice(pc1_corrected, 20, 30))
let pc1_corr_trt = concat(slice(pc1_corrected, 10, 20), slice(pc1_corrected, 30, 40))

let group_test_after = ttest(pc1_corr_ctrl, pc1_corr_trt)
println("Treatment effect in PC1 after correction:")
println("  p-value: " + to_string(round(group_test_after.p_value, 4)))

Part 3: Why You Should Include Batch as a Covariate Instead

There is a philosophical debate in genomics:

Approach A: Correct the data, then analyze. Remove batch effects from the expression matrix, then do differential expression analysis on the corrected data.

Approach B: Include batch as a covariate in the model. Leave the data uncorrected and add batch as a covariate in the statistical model (e.g., DESeq2 design formula: ~ batch + treatment).

Approach B is generally preferred for hypothesis testing because:

  1. It preserves the full statistical model (uncertainty from batch estimation is included in the standard errors)
  2. It does not alter the data (you analyze the original measurements)
  3. It handles continuous confounders (you cannot “correct” for age the way you correct for batch)
  4. It gives correct p-values and confidence intervals

Approach A is useful for:

  • Visualization (PCA plots, heatmaps)
  • Clustering (you want biology-driven clusters)
  • Machine learning (you want batch-free features)
# Demonstrate the two approaches

# Approach A: Correct then analyze
# (Already done above — corrected_data)
let de_corrected = sim_data |> map(|gene|
    let corrected_gene = zip(gene, batch_numeric) |> map(|pair|
        pair[0] - lm(gene, batch_numeric).coefficients[0] * pair[1]
    )
    let ctrl = concat(slice(corrected_gene, 0, 10), slice(corrected_gene, 20, 30))
    let trt = concat(slice(corrected_gene, 10, 20), slice(corrected_gene, 30, 40))
    ttest(ctrl, trt).p_value
)

# Approach B: Include batch as covariate
let de_covariate = sim_data |> map(|gene|
    # Multiple regression: expression ~ treatment + batch
    let model = lm(gene, matrix([group_numeric, batch_numeric]))
    model.p_values[0]  # p-value for treatment effect
)

# Count significant genes with each approach
let sig_corrected = de_corrected |> filter(|p| p < 0.05) |> len()
let sig_covariate = de_covariate |> filter(|p| p < 0.05) |> len()

println("Differential expression results:")
println("  Approach A (correct then test): " + to_string(sig_corrected) + " significant genes")
println("  Approach B (batch as covariate): " + to_string(sig_covariate) + " significant genes")
println("")
println("  Both should give similar results when batch and biology are orthogonal")
println("  Approach B is preferred for formal testing")

Part 4: Confounding — When Batch and Biology Are Entangled

The most dangerous scenario is when batch is correlated with the biological variable of interest. This happens when:

  • All cases were processed in one batch and all controls in another
  • One lab contributed mostly early-stage tumors and another mostly late-stage
  • Different sequencing platforms were used for different groups
# Simulate a CONFOUNDED design
# Batch A: 20 controls
# Batch B: 20 treatments
# Now batch and treatment are perfectly confounded!

let confounded_batch = concat(
    rnorm(20, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Batch_A"),
    rnorm(20, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Batch_B")
)
let confounded_group = concat(
    rnorm(20, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Control"),
    rnorm(20, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| "Treatment")
)

let confounded_batch_num = confounded_batch |> map(|b| if b == "Batch_A" then 0.0 else 1.0)
let confounded_group_num = confounded_group |> map(|g| if g == "Control" then 0.0 else 1.0)

# These are perfectly correlated!
let confound_cor = cor(confounded_batch_num, confounded_group_num)
println("Correlation between batch and treatment: " + to_string(confound_cor))
println("PERFECT CONFOUNDING — cannot separate batch from treatment")
println("")
println("Any gene that differs between batches also differs between groups")
println("Any gene that differs between groups also differs between batches")
println("NO statistical method can disentangle these effects")

# Show that regression fails with perfect confounding
let confounded_gene = concat(
    rnorm(20, 5.0, 1.0),  # Batch A / Control
    rnorm(20, 8.0, 1.0)   # Batch B / Treatment
)

# Is the difference due to batch or treatment?
# We literally cannot tell
let ttest_result = ttest(slice(confounded_gene, 0, 20), slice(confounded_gene, 20, 40))
println("T-test p-value: " + to_string(ttest_result.p_value))
println("  Is this batch or biology? UNKNOWABLE")
println("")
println("Lesson: Confounding must be prevented at the DESIGN stage")
println("  Randomize samples across batches")
println("  If randomization is impossible, ensure batch and biology are")
println("  as uncorrelated as possible")

Part 5: Simpson’s Paradox in Genomics

Simpson’s paradox occurs when a trend that appears in grouped data reverses when the groups are combined. This is a direct consequence of confounding.

# Simpson's paradox example
# Gene X is upregulated in treatment in BOTH batches
# But overall, it appears DOWNregulated in treatment

# Batch A: low overall expression, treatment slightly higher
let batch_a_ctrl = rnorm(30, 3.0, 0.5)
let batch_a_trt = rnorm(10, 3.5, 0.5)

# Batch B: high overall expression, treatment slightly higher
let batch_b_ctrl = rnorm(10, 8.0, 0.5)
let batch_b_trt = rnorm(30, 8.5, 0.5)

# Within each batch: treatment > control
println("Within-batch effects:")
println("  Batch A: Control mean = " + to_string(round(mean(batch_a_ctrl), 2))
    + ", Treatment mean = " + to_string(round(mean(batch_a_trt), 2))
    + " (treatment higher)")
println("  Batch B: Control mean = " + to_string(round(mean(batch_b_ctrl), 2))
    + ", Treatment mean = " + to_string(round(mean(batch_b_trt), 2))
    + " (treatment higher)")

# Combined: control samples come mostly from high-expression Batch B
# treatment samples come mostly from low-expression Batch A
let all_ctrl = concat(batch_a_ctrl, batch_b_ctrl)
let all_trt = concat(batch_a_trt, batch_b_trt)

println("")
println("Combined effect (ignoring batch):")
println("  Control mean = " + to_string(round(mean(all_ctrl), 2))
    + " (n=" + to_string(len(all_ctrl)) + ")")
println("  Treatment mean = " + to_string(round(mean(all_trt), 2))
    + " (n=" + to_string(len(all_trt)) + ")")

let overall_test = ttest(all_ctrl, all_trt)
println("  T-test p-value: " + to_string(round(overall_test.p_value, 4)))
println("")
println("PARADOX: Treatment is HIGHER within each batch")
println("         But LOWER overall because batch confounds the comparison")
println("         More controls came from the high-expression batch")

# The fix: include batch in the model
let combined_expr = concat(concat(batch_a_ctrl, batch_a_trt),
                           concat(batch_b_ctrl, batch_b_trt))
let combined_group = concat(
    concat(rnorm(30, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 0.0),
           rnorm(10, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 1.0)),
    concat(rnorm(10, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 0.0),
           rnorm(30, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 1.0))
)
let combined_batch = concat(
    rnorm(40, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 0.0),
    rnorm(40, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 1.0)
)

let corrected_model = lm(combined_expr, matrix([combined_group, combined_batch]))
println("Multiple regression (expression ~ treatment + batch):")
println("  Treatment coefficient: " + to_string(round(corrected_model.coefficients[0], 4)))
println("  Batch coefficient: " + to_string(round(corrected_model.coefficients[1], 4)))
println("  Treatment p-value: " + to_string(round(corrected_model.p_values[0], 4)))
println("  (Now correctly shows treatment effect within batches)")

Part 6: Population Stratification in GWAS

Population stratification is a confounding problem specific to genetics. If cases and controls come from different ancestral populations, any allele that differs in frequency between populations will appear associated with the disease — even if it has no causal effect.

# Simulate population stratification
# Population A (European): higher frequency of allele, lower disease risk
# Population B (East Asian): lower frequency of allele, higher disease risk

let n_european = 500
let n_east_asian = 500

# Allele frequency differs between populations (not related to disease)
let af_european = 0.30
let af_east_asian = 0.15

# Disease prevalence differs between populations (for other reasons)
let disease_european = 0.05
let disease_east_asian = 0.10

# Generate genotypes
let geno_eur = rbinom(n_european, 2, af_european) |> map(|x| to_float(x))
let geno_eas = rbinom(n_east_asian, 2, af_east_asian) |> map(|x| to_float(x))

# Generate disease status (independent of genotype!)
let disease_eur = rbinom(n_european, 1, disease_european) |> map(|x| to_float(x))
let disease_eas = rbinom(n_east_asian, 1, disease_east_asian) |> map(|x| to_float(x))

let all_geno = concat(geno_eur, geno_eas)
let all_disease = concat(disease_eur, disease_eas)
let all_pop = concat(
    rnorm(n_european, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 0.0),
    rnorm(n_east_asian, 0.0, 0.0) |> map(|_| 1.0)
)

# Naive association (ignoring population)
let naive_chi2 = chi_square(
    [
        all_geno |> filter(|g| g > 0.0) |> len(),
        all_geno |> filter(|g| g == 0.0) |> len()
    ],
    [
        all_disease |> filter(|d| d == 1.0) |> len(),
        all_disease |> filter(|d| d == 0.0) |> len()
    ]
)

# Corrected association (including population as covariate)
let corrected_assoc = glm(all_disease, matrix([all_geno, all_pop]), "binomial")

println("Population stratification example:")
println("  Allele has NO causal effect on disease")
println("  But populations differ in both allele frequency and disease risk")

    + to_string(round(corrected_assoc.p_values[0], 4)))

    println("  The naive test gives a FALSE POSITIVE")
    println("  The corrected test correctly shows no association")
end

# GWAS solves this with principal components
# PCA of genome-wide genotypes captures population structure
# Include top PCs (typically 10-20) as covariates in the association model

Part 8: Detecting Unknown Batches with Surrogate Variables

Sometimes batch information is not recorded or is incomplete. Surrogate Variable Analysis (SVA) uses the expression data itself to detect hidden sources of variation.

# The principle of surrogate variable analysis:
# 1. Fit a model with only the biological variable of interest
# 2. Examine the residuals — they contain batch effects + noise
# 3. Apply PCA/SVD to the residuals
# 4. The top components are "surrogate variables" — proxies for unknown batches
# 5. Include surrogate variables as covariates in the final model

println("Surrogate Variable Analysis (SVA) principle:")
println("  1. Fit expression ~ treatment (ignoring batch)")
println("  2. Residuals = batch effects + noise")
println("  3. PCA of residuals → surrogate variables")
println("  4. Fit expression ~ treatment + SV1 + SV2 + ...")
println("  5. SVs capture unknown batch effects without needing batch labels")
println("")
println("BioLang implementation:")

# Compute residuals from biology-only model
let residual_data = sim_data |> map(|gene|
    let model = lm(gene, group_numeric)
    # Residuals capture everything not explained by treatment
    zip(gene, group_numeric) |> map(|pair|
        pair[0] - (model.intercept + model.coefficients[0] * pair[1])
    )
)

let residual_matrix = matrix(residual_data)
let svd_result = pca(residual_matrix)

println("Surrogate variables from residual PCA:")
println("  SV1 variance explained: " + to_string(round(svd_result.variance_explained[0] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")
println("  SV2 variance explained: " + to_string(round(svd_result.variance_explained[1] * 100.0, 1)) + "%")

# SV1 should correlate with batch if batch is the dominant hidden effect
let sv1 = svd_result.scores |> map(|row| row[0])
let sv1_batch_cor = cor(sv1, batch_numeric |> map(|x| to_float(x)))
println("Correlation of SV1 with actual batch: " + to_string(round(abs(sv1_batch_cor), 4)))
println("  (High correlation confirms SV1 captures the batch effect)")

Interpretation

The Hierarchy of Batch Effect Solutions

From best to worst:

  1. Prevention: Randomize samples across batches at the design stage. This makes batch orthogonal to biology. No correction needed.

  2. Covariate adjustment: Include batch as a covariate in the statistical model. Preserves the data, gives correct p-values, handles both known and estimated (surrogate variable) batch effects.

  3. Data correction: Remove batch effects from the data (ComBat, limma removeBatchEffect). Useful for visualization and clustering but can distort standard errors. Always analyze the uncorrected data with covariates for hypothesis testing.

  4. Ignoring batch effects: Produces false discoveries, missed true signals, and irreproducible results. Never acceptable.

When Correction Fails

Correction fails when:

  • Batch is perfectly confounded with biology (no statistical fix)
  • The batch effect is non-linear (standard methods assume linearity)
  • The batch effect is gene-specific and complex (standard methods assume simple additive/multiplicative effects)
  • Sample sizes per batch are too small for reliable estimation

What We Learned from the Simulations

  1. A batch shift of 2 log2-units dominates PCA, making the biological signal invisible
  2. Library size normalization removes part but not all of the batch effect
  3. Regression-based correction effectively removes the batch effect when batch and biology are orthogonal
  4. When batch and biology are confounded, no correction can recover the biological signal
  5. Simpson’s paradox is a real threat — always check for confounders before interpreting overall trends

When to Abort a Study

Sometimes batch effects are so severe or confounded that the study cannot produce reliable results. Consider abandoning the analysis when:

  • Batch is perfectly confounded with the biological variable
  • Batch effects are so large that biological signal is undetectable even after correction
  • The number of samples per batch is too small (< 5) for reliable batch estimation
  • Key clinical covariates are missing, making it impossible to check for confounding

In these cases, the honest conclusion is: “This study design does not support the intended analysis. New data with a balanced batch design is needed.”

Where Next?

Immediate Extensions

  • Dimensionality reduction (Chapter 13): PCA is just the starting point. UMAP and t-SNE are commonly used for visualization in single-cell RNA-seq, where batch effects between experiments are a major challenge.

  • Clustering (Chapter 14): Batch effects can create artificial clusters. Always check whether clusters correspond to biological groups or technical batches before interpreting them.

  • Meta-analysis (Chapter 15): The principled alternative to combining raw data across studies. Analyze each study separately, then combine effect sizes. This avoids cross-study batch correction entirely.

Advanced Topics

  • Harmony and Scanorama: Batch correction methods designed specifically for single-cell RNA-seq, where each experiment has different cell compositions and capture efficiencies.

  • Mutual nearest neighbors (MNN): A method that identifies shared cell types across batches and uses them as anchors for correction. More principled than global correction for heterogeneous samples.

  • Causal inference: When you cannot randomize (observational studies), methods like propensity score matching, instrumental variables, and Mendelian randomization can help distinguish causal effects from confounding.

Practice Problems

  1. GTEx batch detection: Download GTEx sample-level data (not just medians) and color PCA by sequencing center, ischemic time, and tissue type. Which technical variable explains the most variance?

  2. Simulated confounding: Create a simulation where batch is 50% confounded with treatment (60% of treatments in Batch A, 40% in Batch B). How does partial confounding affect the DE results? How well does covariate adjustment work?

  3. Over-correction: Simulate data where treatment causes a 2-fold change in 100 genes, with batch perfectly correlated with treatment. Apply batch correction and show that the biological signal is removed. Then simulate the balanced design and show that correction preserves the biology.

  4. Population stratification: Using 1000 Genomes Project data, compute PCA of genotypes. Show that the top PCs separate continental populations. Simulate a GWAS with population-stratified cases and controls, and show that PC adjustment removes the inflation.

  5. Library size correlation: Take any RNA-seq count matrix. For each gene, compute its correlation with total library size. How many genes are significantly correlated? Apply TPM or CPM normalization and repeat. How many genes are still correlated?

Key takeaways from this chapter:

  • Batch effects are systematic technical variations that affect all samples processed together. They are present in virtually all genomic data.
  • PCA is the primary tool for detecting batch effects. If PC1 separates batches, the batch effect dominates the biology.
  • Confounding occurs when batch correlates with the biological variable. No statistical method can fix perfect confounding.
  • For hypothesis testing, include batch as a covariate in the model rather than correcting the data.
  • For visualization and clustering, use regression-based or ComBat correction on the data.
  • Simpson’s paradox can reverse the apparent direction of an effect when confounders are ignored.
  • Population stratification is a form of confounding in GWAS, corrected by including ancestry PCs as covariates.
  • Prevention (balanced experimental design) is always better than post-hoc correction.
  • Report batch information, correction methods, and before/after PCA plots in every multi-batch study.
