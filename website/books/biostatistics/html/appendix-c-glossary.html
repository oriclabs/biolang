Biostatistics in Practice

Appendix C: Glossary

A

Adjusted Rand Index (ARI): A measure of agreement between two clusterings, corrected for chance. ARI = 0 means no better than random, ARI = 1 means perfect agreement. Used to compare unsupervised clusters to known labels (e.g., k-means clusters vs PAM50 subtypes). See Chapter 14.

AIC (Akaike Information Criterion): A model selection criterion that balances goodness of fit against model complexity. Lower AIC is better. AIC = 2k - 2ln(L), where k is the number of parameters and L is the likelihood. Prefers simpler models when fit is comparable.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The hypothesis that there is an effect or difference. In a two-sample t-test, the alternative hypothesis is that the two group means are not equal. The p-value is computed under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true.

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance): A statistical test comparing means across three or more groups. Tests whether any group mean differs from the others. The F-statistic is the ratio of between-group variance to within-group variance. BioLang: anova(). See Chapter 6.

B

Batch Effect: A systematic technical artifact that affects measurements from different experimental batches (sequencing lanes, processing dates, laboratories). Batch effects can be larger than biological signal and must be identified and corrected before analysis. See Chapter 12.

Bayes Factor: The ratio of the likelihood of the data under two competing hypotheses. Unlike p-values, Bayes factors directly compare hypotheses and can provide evidence for the null.

Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) Procedure: The standard method for controlling the false discovery rate in multiple testing. Ranks p-values, then finds the largest rank i where p(i) <= i/m * alpha. All tests with rank <= i are called significant. BioLang: p_adjust(pvals, "BH"). See Chapter 8.

BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion): A model selection criterion similar to AIC but with a stronger penalty for model complexity. BIC = k*ln(n) - 2ln(L). Tends to select simpler models than AIC, especially for large n.

Biological Replicate: An independent biological sample (different mouse, different patient, different cell culture). Biological replicates capture biological variability and are essential for valid statistical inference. Contrast with technical replicate.

Bonferroni Correction: The simplest multiple testing correction: multiply each p-value by the number of tests (or equivalently, divide the significance threshold by the number of tests). Controls the family-wise error rate but is very conservative. BioLang: p_adjust(pvals, "bonferroni").

Box Plot: A visualization showing the median, interquartile range (box), and range (whiskers) of a distribution. Useful for comparing distributions across groups. BioLang: boxplot().

C

Censoring: In survival analysis, an observation is censored when the event of interest has not occurred by the end of follow-up. Right censoring (the most common type) means the patient was alive at last contact but their eventual survival time is unknown.

Chi-Squared Test: A test for association between categorical variables, or for goodness of fit to an expected distribution. Compares observed counts to expected counts. BioLang: chi_square(). See Chapter 5.

Clustering: Unsupervised method for grouping observations based on similarity. Common methods: k-means, hierarchical, Leiden. See Chapter 14.

Cohen’s d: A standardized effect size for the difference between two group means. d = (mean1 - mean2) / pooled_SD. Small: d=0.2, medium: d=0.5, large: d=0.8. Independent of sample size, unlike p-values.

Confidence Interval (CI): A range of values that, with a specified probability (typically 95%), contains the true population parameter. A 95% CI means: if you repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the resulting CIs would contain the true value. It does NOT mean there is a 95% probability the true value is in this specific interval.

Confounder: A variable that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, distorting the apparent relationship between them. Example: age confounds the association between gray hair and cancer because age causes both. See Chapter 12.

Correlation: A measure of linear association between two variables. Pearson’s r ranges from -1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive). r=0 means no linear relationship. BioLang: cor(). See Chapter 9.

Covariance Matrix: A matrix where entry (i,j) is the covariance between variables i and j. The diagonal contains the variances. Used in PCA, multivariate tests, and mixed models. BioLang: cov_matrix().

Cox Proportional Hazards: A semi-parametric regression model for time-to-event data. Estimates hazard ratios for each covariate. Assumes proportional hazards (the ratio of hazards is constant over time). BioLang: cox_ph(). See Chapter 11.

D

Degrees of Freedom (df): The number of independent pieces of information used to estimate a parameter. For a t-test with n observations, df = n-1 (or approximately n1+n2-2 for two-sample). More df means more precise estimates and narrower confidence intervals.

Density Plot: A smoothed version of a histogram showing the probability density function. BioLang: histogram().

Differential Expression: Identifying genes whose expression differs between conditions. Uses negative binomial models (DESeq2, edgeR) for count data or linear models for normalized data.

Distance Matrix: A matrix where entry (i,j) is the distance between observations i and j. Used for clustering and dimensionality reduction. BioLang: dist_matrix(). Common metrics: Euclidean, Manhattan, correlation.

E

Effect Size: A quantitative measure of the magnitude of a phenomenon. Examples: Cohen’s d (standardized mean difference), odds ratio, hazard ratio, correlation coefficient. Unlike p-values, effect sizes are independent of sample size.

Eigenvalue: In PCA, the eigenvalue of a principal component equals the variance explained by that component. Eigenvalues are obtained from the eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix. BioLang: eigenvalues().

Elbow Plot (Scree Plot): A plot of eigenvalues vs PC number used to choose the number of principal components. The “elbow” is where the curve flattens, indicating that additional PCs capture mostly noise.

Enrichment Analysis: Testing whether a set of genes (e.g., differentially expressed genes) is enriched for a particular pathway or GO term beyond what would be expected by chance. BioLang: enrich(). Uses Fisher’s exact test or hypergeometric test.

F

False Discovery Rate (FDR): The expected proportion of false positives among all rejected hypotheses. FDR = 5% means that among all genes called significant, 5% are expected to be false positives. Controlled by the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure. See Chapter 8.

Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER): The probability of making at least one false positive across all tests. More conservative than FDR. Controlled by Bonferroni or Holm corrections.

Fisher’s Exact Test: An exact test for association in a 2x2 contingency table. Unlike the chi-squared test, it is valid for small sample sizes. BioLang: fisher_exact().

Fixed-Effects Model: In meta-analysis, assumes all studies estimate the same true effect. Appropriate when heterogeneity is low (I-squared < 25%). See Chapter 15.

Fold Change: The ratio of expression in one condition to another. A fold change of 2 means the gene is twice as highly expressed. Often reported on the log2 scale: log2FC = 1 corresponds to a 2-fold increase.

Forest Plot: The standard meta-analysis visualization showing each study’s estimate with confidence interval, plus the pooled estimate. BioLang: forest_plot(). See Chapter 15.

G

GLM (Generalized Linear Model): An extension of linear regression to non-normal outcomes. Includes logistic regression (binary outcome), Poisson regression (count outcome), and others. Uses a link function to relate the linear predictor to the mean. BioLang: glm(). See Chapter 10.

H

Hazard Ratio (HR): In survival analysis, the ratio of hazard rates between two groups. HR=2 means the event rate is twice as high in one group. HR=1 means no difference. Estimated by Cox proportional hazards model.

Heatmap: A matrix visualization where values are represented by color intensity. Used to display expression matrices, correlation matrices, and distance matrices. BioLang: heatmap().

Heterogeneity: In meta-analysis, the degree to which true effect sizes vary across studies. Measured by I-squared and Cochran’s Q. High heterogeneity (I-squared > 75%) suggests the studies are measuring different things.

Histogram: A visualization showing the frequency distribution of a continuous variable. BioLang: histogram().

I

I-Squared (I2): The percentage of total variability in a meta-analysis that is due to true between-study heterogeneity (rather than sampling error). 0% = no heterogeneity, 25% = low, 50% = moderate, 75% = high. See Chapter 15.

Independent Filtering: In multiple testing, removing tests with low overall signal (e.g., low-expression genes) before FDR correction. This increases power by reducing the number of tests without biasing the results. See Chapter 8.

K

Kaplan-Meier Estimator: A non-parametric method for estimating the survival function from time-to-event data with censoring. Produces a step function that decreases at each event time. BioLang: kaplan_meier(). See Chapter 11.

K-Means Clustering: An unsupervised method that partitions n observations into k clusters by minimizing within-cluster sum of squares. Requires specifying k in advance. Finds spherical clusters. BioLang: kmeans(). See Chapter 14.

L

Leiden Algorithm: A community detection algorithm for graph-based clustering. Widely used in single-cell genomics. Does not require specifying the number of clusters; uses a resolution parameter instead. BioLang: leiden(). See Chapter 14.

Linear Regression: Models the relationship between a continuous outcome and one or more predictors as a linear function. Estimates slope (effect size), intercept, and R-squared. BioLang: lm(). See Chapter 9.

Link Function: In GLMs, the function that relates the linear predictor to the expected value of the outcome. Logistic regression uses the logit link; Poisson regression uses the log link; linear regression uses the identity link.

Log Transformation: Applying the logarithm (usually log2 or log10) to data. Common in genomics because expression data spans several orders of magnitude and raw counts are right-skewed. Log-transformed data is often approximately normal.

M

Meta-Analysis: The statistical method for combining results from multiple independent studies. Produces a pooled effect estimate with proper uncertainty. See Chapter 15.

Multiple Testing Problem: When performing many simultaneous tests (e.g., testing 20,000 genes), the probability of at least one false positive increases dramatically. At alpha=0.05 with 20,000 tests, expect 1,000 false positives by chance. Must be corrected using FDR or FWER methods. See Chapter 8.

N

Negative Binomial Distribution: A count distribution that allows variance to exceed the mean (overdispersion). The standard model for RNA-seq count data. Used by DESeq2 and edgeR.

Non-Parametric Test: A statistical test that makes no assumptions about the distribution of the data. Examples: Wilcoxon rank-sum, Spearman correlation, Kruskal-Wallis. Generally less powerful than parametric tests when assumptions hold, but more robust when they do not.

Normal Distribution: See Appendix B. The bell curve, parameterized by mean and standard deviation.

Null Hypothesis (H0): The hypothesis of no effect or no difference. In a t-test comparing treatment vs control, H0 is that the true means are equal. The p-value is the probability of observing data as extreme as yours if H0 is true.

O

Odds Ratio (OR): The ratio of the odds of an event in one group to the odds in another. OR=1 means no association. OR>1 means higher odds in the first group. Used in case-control studies and logistic regression.

Overdispersion: When the observed variance exceeds the variance predicted by a model. Common in count data: RNA-seq counts have more variance than the Poisson distribution predicts. Addressed by using the negative binomial distribution.

P

p-value: The probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as (or more extreme than) the one computed from the data, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It is NOT the probability that the null hypothesis is true. It is NOT the probability that the result is due to chance.

Parametric Test: A statistical test that assumes a specific distribution for the data (usually normal). Examples: t-test, ANOVA, Pearson correlation. More powerful than non-parametric tests when assumptions hold.

PCA (Principal Component Analysis): A dimensionality reduction method that finds orthogonal directions of maximum variance. BioLang: pca(). See Chapter 13.

Poisson Distribution: See Appendix B. A count distribution where mean equals variance. Used for modeling rare events.

Power: The probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis when it is false (i.e., detecting a true effect). Power = 1 - Type II error rate. Depends on sample size, effect size, significance level, and variability. See Chapter 4.

Pseudoreplication: Treating non-independent observations as independent replicates. Example: measuring the same mouse 5 times and calling it n=5 instead of n=1 with 5 technical replicates. Inflates sample size and produces falsely significant p-values.

Publication Bias: The tendency for studies with significant results to be published while null results remain unpublished. Biases meta-analyses toward overestimating effect sizes.

Q

QQ Plot (Quantile-Quantile Plot): A plot comparing observed quantiles to theoretical quantiles (usually normal or uniform). Points on the diagonal indicate the data follows the theoretical distribution. Deviations reveal non-normality, inflation, or true signal. BioLang: qq_plot().

R

R-Squared (R2): The proportion of variance in the outcome explained by the predictor(s). R2 = 0 means no explanatory power, R2 = 1 means perfect prediction. In genomics, R2 is often small (0.01-0.10 for individual SNPs) because genetic effects are typically modest.

Random-Effects Model: In meta-analysis, assumes that true effect sizes vary between studies according to a distribution. Produces wider confidence intervals than fixed-effects. Appropriate when I-squared > 25%. See Chapter 15.

Residual: The difference between an observed value and the value predicted by a model. Examining residuals reveals model misspecification (patterns in residuals indicate the model is wrong).

S

Sensitivity: The probability of a positive test result given that the condition is truly present. Also called the true positive rate or recall. Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN).

Silhouette Score: A measure of cluster quality. For each observation, it compares the average distance to same-cluster points vs the average distance to the nearest other cluster. Ranges from -1 (wrong cluster) to +1 (well-clustered). See Chapter 14.

Specificity: The probability of a negative test result given that the condition is truly absent. Also called the true negative rate. Specificity = TN / (TN + FP).

Standard Error (SE): The standard deviation of a sampling distribution. For the sample mean, SE = SD / sqrt(n). Measures the precision of an estimate. Smaller SE = more precise estimate.

Survival Analysis: Statistical methods for analyzing time-to-event data, accounting for censoring. Key methods: Kaplan-Meier curves, log-rank test, Cox proportional hazards. BioLang: kaplan_meier(), cox_ph(). See Chapter 11.

T

t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding): A nonlinear dimensionality reduction method for visualization. Preserves local structure but NOT global distances or cluster sizes. BioLang: tsne(). See Chapter 13.

Technical Replicate: Repeated measurements of the same biological sample (same RNA extraction sequenced twice, same sample run on two lanes). Technical replicates measure measurement precision but do NOT capture biological variability. They cannot substitute for biological replicates in statistical tests.

Type I Error (False Positive): Rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true. The significance level (alpha) is the maximum acceptable Type I error rate. Alpha = 0.05 means you accept a 5% chance of a false positive.

Type II Error (False Negative): Failing to reject the null hypothesis when it is actually false. Beta is the Type II error rate. Power = 1 - beta.

U

UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection): A nonlinear dimensionality reduction method that preserves both local and some global structure. Faster than t-SNE and more reproducible. Standard for single-cell visualization. BioLang: umap(). See Chapter 13.

V

Variance: The average squared deviation from the mean. Measures the spread of a distribution. BioLang: variance(). Standard deviation is the square root of variance.

Volcano Plot: A scatter plot of effect size (log2 fold change) vs significance (-log10 p-value). Points in the upper corners are both statistically significant and biologically meaningful. BioLang: volcano().

W

Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test (Mann-Whitney U): A non-parametric test for comparing two independent groups. Ranks all observations and compares the sum of ranks between groups. Does not assume normality. BioLang: wilcoxon(). See Chapter 7.

Winner’s Curse: The phenomenon where the effect size estimated in a discovery study is systematically larger than the true effect. Occurs because studies that detect significant effects (and get published) tend to overestimate the effect size. Replication studies typically show smaller effects. See Chapter 15.
