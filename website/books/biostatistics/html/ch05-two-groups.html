Biostatistics in Practice

Comparing Two Groups

“The difference between a significant result and a non-significant result is not itself statistically significant.” — Andrew Gelman & Hal Stern

You have gene expression measurements from tumor tissue and normal tissue. The question seems simple: which genes are different? The answer is anything but.

This chapter teaches you to compare two groups properly — with the right test, the right assumptions check, the right effect size measure, and the right visualization. We will work with real breast cancer expression data and discover that p-values alone are dangerously incomplete.

The Problem

A breast cancer researcher has RNA-seq expression data from tumor and adjacent normal tissue. For each of ~20,000 genes, she has FPKM values in both conditions. She wants to identify genes that are differentially expressed between tumor and normal.

Her first instinct: run a t-test on every gene, keep the ones with p < 0.05.

This approach has at least five problems. By the end of this chapter, you will understand all of them and know how to do it correctly.

Why It Matters

Comparing two groups is the most common statistical task in biology. Tumor vs normal. Treated vs untreated. Responder vs non-responder. Wild-type vs knockout. Case vs control.

Getting this comparison wrong has real consequences:

  • False positives waste years of follow-up experiments on genes that are not truly different
  • False negatives miss real biological signals — the drug target that was actually there
  • Ignoring effect size means you chase genes with tiny differences that are statistically significant only because of large sample size
  • Ignoring assumptions means the p-values you report are not calibrated — they do not mean what you think they mean

The t-test was invented in 1908 by William Sealy Gosset (publishing under the pseudonym “Student”) while working at the Guinness Brewery. He needed to compare small samples of barley yields. Over a century later, biologists still misuse his test daily.

The Data

We use GEO dataset GSE183947, which contains FPKM expression values from breast cancer (tumor) and adjacent normal tissue samples. This is a real RNA-seq dataset with paired tumor-normal samples.

The Log-Transform Question

Gene expression data is almost always right-skewed — a few genes have very high expression, most are moderate or low. This violates the normality assumption of the t-test.

# Raw expression is typically right-skewed
# Check skewness by comparing mean vs median
let skew_ratio = mean(tp53_tumor) / median(tp53_tumor)
println("Mean/median ratio (tumor): " + to_string(round(skew_ratio, 3)))
# If ratio >> 1, the data is right-skewed

The standard solution is to log-transform:

# Log2-transform with pseudocount to avoid log(0)
let log_tp53_tumor = tp53_tumor |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))
let log_tp53_normal = tp53_normal |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))

println("Log2 tumor mean:   " + to_string(round(mean(log_tp53_tumor), 3)))
println("Log2 tumor median: " + to_string(round(median(log_tp53_tumor), 3)))
println("Log2 normal mean:  " + to_string(round(mean(log_tp53_normal), 3)))
println("Log2 normal median:" + to_string(round(median(log_tp53_normal), 3)))

# QQ plot to check normality after log-transform
qq_plot(log_tp53_tumor, "TP53 Tumor (log2)")
  |> save_plot("tp53_tumor_qq.svg")

qq_plot(log_tp53_normal, "TP53 Normal (log2)")
  |> save_plot("tp53_normal_qq.svg")

A QQ plot plots the quantiles of your data against the quantiles of a normal distribution. If the points fall on a straight line, the data is approximately normal. Deviations at the tails indicate heavy or light tails.

Rule of thumb: Log-transformed expression data is usually close enough to normal for the t-test. Always check with a QQ plot.

The Analysis

Step 1: Student’s t-Test — The Classic

Student’s t-test assumes:

  1. Both groups are drawn from normal distributions
  2. Both groups have equal variance (homoscedasticity)
  3. Observations are independent
# Student's t-test on log-transformed TP53
let result = ttest(log_tp53_tumor, log_tp53_normal)
println("=== Student's t-test (TP53 log2-FPKM) ===")
println("t-statistic: " + to_string(round(result.statistic, 4)))
println("p-value:     " + to_string(result.p_value))
println("df:          " + to_string(round(result.df, 1)))

But wait — did we check the equal variance assumption?

# Check variance ratio (F-ratio)
let var_tumor = variance(log_tp53_tumor)
let var_normal = variance(log_tp53_normal)
let f_ratio = var_tumor / var_normal
println("Variance tumor:  " + to_string(round(var_tumor, 4)))
println("Variance normal: " + to_string(round(var_normal, 4)))
println("F-ratio:         " + to_string(round(f_ratio, 4)))
# If F-ratio is far from 1 (say > 2 or < 0.5), variances are unequal

Step 2: Welch’s t-Test — The Safer Choice

Welch’s t-test does not assume equal variance. It adjusts the degrees of freedom based on the sample variances. In practice, Welch’s test is almost always preferred because:

  • When variances are equal, Welch’s gives nearly identical results to Student’s
  • When variances are unequal, Student’s gives wrong p-values but Welch’s is correct
  • There is almost no cost to using Welch’s and a real cost to using Student’s incorrectly
# Welch's t-test (default in most modern software)
# In BioLang, ttest performs Welch's by default
let welch = ttest(log_tp53_tumor, log_tp53_normal)
println("=== Welch's t-test (TP53 log2-FPKM) ===")
println("t-statistic: " + to_string(round(welch.statistic, 4)))
println("p-value:     " + to_string(welch.p_value))
println("df:          " + to_string(round(welch.df, 1)))

Key insight: The degrees of freedom in Welch’s test are often not an integer. This is correct — it is the Welch-Satterthwaite approximation. If you see df = 14.7 instead of df = 18, that is Welch’s adjustment working.

Step 3: When to Use Paired Tests

If each tumor sample has a matched normal sample from the same patient, you should use a paired t-test. Pairing removes patient-to-patient variability and dramatically increases statistical power.

# If samples are paired (tumor-normal from same patient):
# The paired test operates on DIFFERENCES within each pair
let differences = zip(log_tp53_tumor, log_tp53_normal)
  |> map(|pair| pair[0] - pair[1])

println("Mean of differences: " + to_string(round(mean(differences), 4)))
println("SD of differences:   " + to_string(round(stdev(differences), 4)))

# The paired t-test tests whether the mean difference is zero
let paired_result = ttest_paired(log_tp53_tumor, log_tp53_normal)
println("=== Paired t-test (TP53 log2-FPKM) ===")
println("t-statistic: " + to_string(round(paired_result.statistic, 4)))
println("p-value:     " + to_string(paired_result.p_value))

When to pair: If the samples are naturally matched (same patient, same litter, same batch processed together), ALWAYS use the paired test. The unpaired test ignores this information and throws away statistical power.

When NOT to pair: If the samples are independent (different patients, no natural matching), the paired test is inappropriate and can give wrong results.

Step 4: Effect Size — Cohen’s d

A p-value tells you whether a difference is statistically distinguishable from zero. It does NOT tell you how large the difference is. With a large enough sample size, even a biologically meaningless 0.01-FPKM difference becomes “statistically significant.”

Cohen’s d measures effect size in units of standard deviation:

# Cohen's d for independent samples
# d = (mean1 - mean2) / pooled_sd
let mean_diff = mean(log_tp53_tumor) - mean(log_tp53_normal)
let n1 = to_float(len(log_tp53_tumor))
let n2 = to_float(len(log_tp53_normal))
let pooled_var = ((n1 - 1.0) * variance(log_tp53_tumor) + (n2 - 1.0) * variance(log_tp53_normal)) / (n1 + n2 - 2.0)
let pooled_sd = sqrt(pooled_var)
let cohens_d = mean_diff / pooled_sd

println("=== Effect Size ===")
println("Mean difference (log2): " + to_string(round(mean_diff, 4)))
println("Pooled SD:              " + to_string(round(pooled_sd, 4)))
println("Cohen's d:              " + to_string(round(cohens_d, 4)))

Interpreting Cohen’s d:

  • |d| < 0.2: negligible effect
  • |d| ~ 0.2: small effect
  • |d| ~ 0.5: medium effect
  • |d| ~ 0.8: large effect
  • |d| > 1.0: very large effect
# Effect size interpretation
let d_abs = abs(cohens_d)
let interpretation = if d_abs < 0.2 then "negligible"
  else if d_abs < 0.5 then "small"
  else if d_abs < 0.8 then "medium"
  else "large"

println("Effect size interpretation: " + interpretation)

Critical point: A gene with p = 0.001 and d = 0.1 is probably not biologically interesting. A gene with p = 0.06 and d = 1.2 probably is. Report both.

Step 5: Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval gives you a range of plausible values for the true difference. Unlike a p-value, it tells you about the magnitude of the effect.

# 95% confidence interval for the difference in means
let se_diff = sqrt(variance(log_tp53_tumor) / n1 + variance(log_tp53_normal) / n2)
let t_crit = qnorm(0.975)  # approximate for large samples
let ci_lower = mean_diff - t_crit * se_diff
let ci_upper = mean_diff + t_crit * se_diff

println("=== 95% Confidence Interval ===")
println("Difference in means (log2): " + to_string(round(mean_diff, 4)))
println("SE of difference:           " + to_string(round(se_diff, 4)))
println("95% CI: [" + to_string(round(ci_lower, 4)) + ", " + to_string(round(ci_upper, 4)) + "]")

If the confidence interval contains zero, the difference is not statistically significant at the 0.05 level. But notice how much MORE information the CI provides compared to a binary yes/no p-value.

Step 6: The Fold Change

In genomics, the fold change is the ratio of expression between conditions. On log2-transformed data, the fold change is simply the difference:

# Log2 fold change
let log2fc = mean(log_tp53_tumor) - mean(log_tp53_normal)
println("log2 fold change: " + to_string(round(log2fc, 4)))

# Convert back to fold change
let fc = pow(2.0, abs(log2fc))
let direction = if log2fc > 0.0 then "upregulated in tumor" else "downregulated in tumor"
println("Fold change: " + to_string(round(fc, 2)) + "x " + direction)

Step 7: Genome-Wide Analysis

Now let us scale up to all genes. This is where things get interesting.

# Run t-tests for every gene across the expression matrix
let gene_col = columns(expr) |> take(1)  # first column is gene ID
let genes = col_values(expr, gene_col[0])

# Build results table gene by gene
let results = genes |> map(|gene| {
  let row = expr |> filter_by(gene_col[0], |g| g == gene)

  # Extract tumor and normal values
  let tumor_vals = tumor_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()
  let normal_vals = normal_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()

  # Log2 transform
  let log_tumor = tumor_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))
  let log_normal = normal_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))

  # Calculate statistics
  let log2fc = mean(log_tumor) - mean(log_normal)
  let test_result = ttest(log_tumor, log_normal)

  # Cohen's d
  let n_t = to_float(len(log_tumor))
  let n_n = to_float(len(log_normal))
  let pvar = ((n_t - 1.0) * variance(log_tumor) + (n_n - 1.0) * variance(log_normal)) / (n_t + n_n - 2.0)
  let d = if pvar > 0.0 then log2fc / sqrt(pvar) else 0.0

  record(
    "gene", gene,
    "log2fc", round(log2fc, 4),
    "t_stat", round(test_result.statistic, 4),
    "p_value", test_result.p_value,
    "cohens_d", round(d, 4),
    "mean_tumor", round(mean(log_tumor), 4),
    "mean_normal", round(mean(log_normal), 4)
  )
}) |> to_list()

let results_table = table(results)
println("Tested " + to_string(len(results_table)) + " genes")

# Sort by p-value to see top hits
let top_genes = results_table
  |> arrange("p_value")
  |> take(20)

println("=== Top 20 Genes by P-value ===")
top_genes

Step 8: The Volcano Plot

The volcano plot is the signature visualization of differential expression analysis. It plots log2 fold change (x-axis) against -log10 p-value (y-axis). Genes that are both statistically significant AND have large effect size appear in the upper corners.

# Create the volcano plot
let volcano_data = results_table
  |> mutate("neg_log10_p", |row| -log10(row.p_value + 1e-300))

volcano(volcano_data, "log2fc", "neg_log10_p")
  |> save_plot("breast_cancer_volcano.svg")

# Annotated volcano with significance thresholds
# Genes with |log2FC| > 1 AND p < 0.05 are "significant"
let sig_up = results_table
  |> filter_by("log2fc", |fc| fc > 1.0)
  |> filter_by("p_value", |p| p < 0.05)

let sig_down = results_table
  |> filter_by("log2fc", |fc| fc < -1.0)
  |> filter_by("p_value", |p| p < 0.05)

println("Upregulated (FC>2, p<0.05):   " + to_string(len(sig_up)))
println("Downregulated (FC<0.5, p<0.05): " + to_string(len(sig_down)))
println("Not significant:                " + to_string(
  len(results_table) - len(sig_up) - len(sig_down)
))

Step 9: Mann-Whitney U (Wilcoxon Rank-Sum) as Alternative

When the normality assumption is questionable, the Wilcoxon rank-sum test is a non-parametric alternative. It tests whether one group tends to have larger values than the other, without assuming any particular distribution shape.

# Wilcoxon rank-sum test on TP53
let wilcox_result = wilcoxon(log_tp53_tumor, log_tp53_normal)
println("=== Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test (TP53 log2-FPKM) ===")
println("W statistic: " + to_string(wilcox_result.statistic))
println("p-value:     " + to_string(wilcox_result.p_value))

Step 10: Head-to-Head — t-Test vs Wilcoxon

This is the real question: when do parametric and non-parametric tests disagree?

# Compare t-test and Wilcoxon across all genes
let comparison = genes |> map(|gene| {
  let row = expr |> filter_by(gene_col[0], |g| g == gene)
  let tumor_vals = tumor_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()
  let normal_vals = normal_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()

  let log_tumor = tumor_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))
  let log_normal = normal_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))

  let t_result = ttest(log_tumor, log_normal)
  let w_result = wilcoxon(log_tumor, log_normal)

  record(
    "gene", gene,
    "p_ttest", t_result.p_value,
    "p_wilcoxon", w_result.p_value
  )
}) |> to_list()

let comp_table = table(comparison)

# Scatter plot: t-test p-values vs Wilcoxon p-values
let comp_log = comp_table
  |> mutate("log_p_t", |row| -log10(row.p_ttest + 1e-300))
  |> mutate("log_p_w", |row| -log10(row.p_wilcoxon + 1e-300))

scatter(comp_log, "log_p_t", "log_p_w")
  |> save_plot("ttest_vs_wilcoxon.svg")

# Find genes where the tests disagree
# Significant by t-test but not by Wilcoxon
let t_only = comp_table
  |> filter_by("p_ttest", |p| p < 0.05)
  |> filter_by("p_wilcoxon", |p| p >= 0.05)

# Significant by Wilcoxon but not by t-test
let w_only = comp_table
  |> filter_by("p_wilcoxon", |p| p < 0.05)
  |> filter_by("p_ttest", |p| p >= 0.05)

println("=== Disagreements ===")
println("Significant by t-test only:   " + to_string(len(t_only)))
println("Significant by Wilcoxon only: " + to_string(len(w_only)))
println("Agree on significance:        " + to_string(
  len(comp_table) - len(t_only) - len(w_only)
))

Why do they disagree?

The t-test is sensitive to outliers because it uses means and variances. A single extreme value can pull the mean and inflate the variance, changing the p-value substantially.

The Wilcoxon test uses ranks instead of raw values. An outlier that is 10x larger than the next value gets rank n regardless — its magnitude does not matter. This makes it robust to outliers but less powerful when the data truly is normal.

# Examine a gene where they disagree
let disagreement_genes = col_values(t_only, "gene")
if len(disagreement_genes) > 0 then {
  let check_gene = disagreement_genes[0]
  let row = expr |> filter_by(gene_col[0], |g| g == check_gene)
  let tumor_vals = tumor_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()
  let normal_vals = normal_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()

  let log_tumor = tumor_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))
  let log_normal = normal_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))

  println("Gene: " + check_gene)
  println("Tumor values (log2): " + to_string(log_tumor))
  println("Normal values (log2): " + to_string(log_normal))

  # Check for outliers — values more than 3 SDs from mean
  let t_mean = mean(log_tumor)
  let t_sd = stdev(log_tumor)
  let outliers = log_tumor |> filter(|x| abs(x - t_mean) > 3.0 * t_sd)
  println("Outliers in tumor: " + to_string(len(outliers)))

  # Box plot to visualize
  let check_data = table(
    "expression", concat(log_tumor, log_normal),
    "group", concat(
      log_tumor |> map(|_| "Tumor"),
      log_normal |> map(|_| "Normal")
    )
  )
  boxplot(check_data, "group", "expression")
    |> save_plot("disagreement_gene_boxplot.svg")
}

Step 11: The One-Sample t-Test

Sometimes you want to test whether a single group differs from a known value. For instance: is the average log2 fold change across a pathway significantly different from zero?

# One-sample t-test: are the fold changes in a gene set different from zero?
# Let us test a set of known breast cancer genes
let bc_genes = ["BRCA1", "BRCA2", "TP53", "ERBB2", "ESR1", "PGR", "MKI67", "CDH1"]

let bc_fcs = bc_genes |> map(|gene| {
  let row = results_table |> filter_by("gene", |g| contains(g, gene))
  if len(row) > 0 then col_values(row, "log2fc")[0] else 0.0
}) |> filter(|x| x != 0.0)

println("Fold changes of BC genes: " + to_string(bc_fcs))
println("Mean log2FC: " + to_string(round(mean(bc_fcs), 4)))

# Test: is the mean FC significantly different from 0?
let one_sample = ttest_one(bc_fcs, 0.0)
println("=== One-sample t-test ===")
println("t-statistic: " + to_string(round(one_sample.statistic, 4)))
println("p-value:     " + to_string(one_sample.p_value))

Interpretation

What the P-value Actually Means

The p-value is the probability of observing data as extreme as (or more extreme than) what was observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It is NOT:

  • The probability that the null hypothesis is true
  • The probability that you made an error
  • The probability that the result will replicate

A p-value of 0.03 means: “If there were truly no difference between tumor and normal, data this extreme would occur about 3% of the time by chance.”

The Complete Report for a Gene

Never report just a p-value. A complete comparison includes:

# Complete report for TP53
let report_gene = "TP53"
let row = results_table |> filter_by("gene", |g| contains(g, report_gene))

println("=== Complete Report: " + report_gene + " ===")
println("Mean expression (log2 FPKM):")
println("  Tumor:  " + to_string(col_values(row, "mean_tumor")[0]))
println("  Normal: " + to_string(col_values(row, "mean_normal")[0]))
println("Log2 fold change: " + to_string(col_values(row, "log2fc")[0]))
println("Fold change: " + to_string(round(pow(2.0, abs(col_values(row, "log2fc")[0])), 2)) + "x")
println("Direction: " + if col_values(row, "log2fc")[0] > 0.0 then "up in tumor" else "down in tumor")
println("t-statistic: " + to_string(col_values(row, "t_stat")[0]))
println("p-value: " + to_string(col_values(row, "p_value")[0]))
println("Cohen's d: " + to_string(col_values(row, "cohens_d")[0]))
println("Note: p-value is NOT corrected for multiple testing (see Chapter 8)")

When the t-Test Finds Significance but the Biology Does Not

# Find genes with tiny fold changes but "significant" p-values
let stat_only = results_table
  |> filter_by("p_value", |p| p < 0.01)
  |> filter_by("log2fc", |fc| abs(fc) < 0.3)
  |> arrange("p_value")

println("=== Statistically Significant but Biologically Questionable ===")
println("Genes with p < 0.01 but |log2FC| < 0.3:")
println("(These are less than 1.23-fold changes)")
take(stat_only, 10)

These genes are statistically different but the difference is so small it is unlikely to be biologically meaningful. This is exactly why effect size matters.

When Biology Happens Without Statistical Significance

# Find genes with large fold changes but non-significant p-values
let bio_only = results_table
  |> filter_by("p_value", |p| p > 0.05)
  |> filter_by("log2fc", |fc| abs(fc) > 2.0)
  |> arrange("log2fc")

println("=== Biologically Large but Not Statistically Significant ===")
println("Genes with p > 0.05 but |log2FC| > 2 (4-fold change):")
take(bio_only, 10)

These genes have large fold changes but high variance or small sample sizes prevent the difference from being statistically significant. They may be worth investigating with more samples.

Decision

What Test Should You Use?

# Decision framework encoded in code:

# 1. Are the samples paired (same patient)?
let paired = true  # set based on experimental design

# 2. Check normality of the data (or log-data)
let check_normality = true  # QQ plot looked reasonable

# 3. Choose the test
let test_choice = if paired then {
  if check_normality then "paired t-test"
  else "Wilcoxon signed-rank"
} else {
  if check_normality then "Welch's t-test"
  else "Wilcoxon rank-sum"
}

println("Recommended test: " + test_choice)

The Recommended Workflow

# Complete workflow for comparing two groups in genomics:

# 1. Log-transform expression data
# 2. Check normality with QQ plots
# 3. Check variance equality (compare SDs)
# 4. Use paired test if samples are matched
# 5. Use Welch's t-test as default (not Student's)
# 6. Always compute Cohen's d
# 7. Always compute confidence intervals
# 8. Always make a volcano plot
# 9. Do NOT interpret raw p-values when testing many genes
#    (see Chapter 8 for multiple testing correction)

Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Using Student’s t-Test When Variances Are Unequal

# Simulate unequal variances
let group_a = rnorm(30, 5.0, 1.0)   # mean=5, sd=1
let group_b = rnorm(30, 5.5, 3.0)   # mean=5.5, sd=3

# Student's t-test ASSUMES equal variance
# Welch's does not
let t_student = ttest(group_a, group_b)
println("Welch's p-value: " + to_string(t_student.p_value))

# With unequal n AND unequal variance, Student's can be seriously wrong
let small_a = rnorm(10, 5.0, 1.0)
let large_b = rnorm(50, 5.5, 3.0)
let t_wrong = ttest(small_a, large_b)
println("Unequal n + unequal var: p = " + to_string(t_wrong.p_value))

Rule: Just use Welch’s t-test. Always. There is no good reason to use Student’s t-test in practice.

Pitfall 2: Testing Without Log-Transforming

# Demonstrate the danger of testing raw FPKM values
# Raw expression is heavily right-skewed
let raw_result = ttest(tp53_tumor, tp53_normal)
let log_result = ttest(log_tp53_tumor, log_tp53_normal)

println("=== Raw vs Log-transformed ===")
println("Raw FPKM t-test p-value: " + to_string(raw_result.p_value))
println("Log2 FPKM t-test p-value: " + to_string(log_result.p_value))
println("These can differ substantially!")

Pitfall 3: Confusing Statistical and Biological Significance

A gene with p = 1e-10 and fold change = 1.05 is statistically significant but biologically irrelevant. With enough samples, ANY non-zero difference becomes significant. Always report and filter by effect size.

Pitfall 4: Ignoring Multiple Testing

# Running 20,000 t-tests without correction
let n_tests = len(results_table)
let n_sig_raw = results_table
  |> filter_by("p_value", |p| p < 0.05)
  |> len()

let expected_false_pos = to_float(n_tests) * 0.05

println("=== The Multiple Testing Problem ===")
println("Total genes tested: " + to_string(n_tests))
println("Significant at p<0.05: " + to_string(n_sig_raw))
println("Expected false positives by chance: " + to_string(round(expected_false_pos, 0)))
println("See Chapter 8 for correction methods!")

Pitfall 5: Applying Paired Test to Unpaired Data (or Vice Versa)

# Paired test on unpaired data gives WRONG results
# If samples are not truly paired, the differences are meaningless

# Simulate: generate two independent groups
let independent_a = rnorm(20, 10.0, 2.0)
let independent_b = rnorm(20, 11.0, 2.0)

# Wrong: treating independent data as paired
let wrong_paired = ttest_paired(independent_a, independent_b)

# Correct: unpaired test
let correct_unpaired = ttest(independent_a, independent_b)

println("Wrong (paired on unpaired data): p = " + to_string(wrong_paired.p_value))
println("Correct (unpaired test):         p = " + to_string(correct_unpaired.p_value))

Pitfall 6: Cherry-Picking Genes

If you look at a gene because it has a large fold change and then test it, you are not doing an honest hypothesis test. You are confirming what you already saw in the data. This inflates false positive rates. Either test all genes (and correct for multiple testing) or have a pre-specified hypothesis.

Pitfall 7: The Pseudocount Matters

# The choice of pseudocount affects log2 fold change for lowly expressed genes
let low_expr = [0.0, 0.0, 0.1, 0.0, 0.2]

let log_pc1 = low_expr |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))
let log_pc01 = low_expr |> map(|x| log2(x + 0.1))
let log_pc001 = low_expr |> map(|x| log2(x + 0.01))

println("Pseudocount = 1:    " + to_string(log_pc1))
println("Pseudocount = 0.1:  " + to_string(log_pc01))
println("Pseudocount = 0.01: " + to_string(log_pc001))
println("The pseudocount dramatically affects near-zero genes!")

Putting It All Together

Here is the complete analysis pipeline for comparing tumor vs normal expression:

# ============================================================
# Complete Two-Group Comparison Pipeline
# ============================================================

# 1. Load data
let url = "https://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/series/GSE183nnn/GSE183947/suppl/GSE183947_fpkm.csv.gz"
let expr = download(url, "GSE183947_fpkm.csv.gz") |> read_csv()

# 2. Identify groups
let all_cols = columns(expr)
let tumor_cols = all_cols |> filter(|c| contains(c, "tumor"))
let normal_cols = all_cols |> filter(|c| contains(c, "normal"))
let gene_col = all_cols[0]

# 3. Genome-wide comparison
let results = col_values(expr, gene_col) |> map(|gene| {
  let row = expr |> filter_by(gene_col, |g| g == gene)

  let tumor_vals = tumor_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()
  let normal_vals = normal_cols |> map(|c| col_values(row, c)) |> flatten()

  # Log-transform
  let lt = tumor_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))
  let ln = normal_vals |> map(|x| log2(x + 1.0))

  # Test and effect size
  let log2fc = mean(lt) - mean(ln)
  let t_res = ttest(lt, ln)
  let w_res = wilcoxon(lt, ln)

  let nt = to_float(len(lt))
  let nn = to_float(len(ln))
  let pv = ((nt - 1.0) * variance(lt) + (nn - 1.0) * variance(ln)) / (nt + nn - 2.0)
  let d = if pv > 0.0 then log2fc / sqrt(pv) else 0.0

  record(
    "gene", gene,
    "log2fc", round(log2fc, 4),
    "p_ttest", t_res.p_value,
    "p_wilcoxon", w_res.p_value,
    "cohens_d", round(d, 4)
  )
}) |> to_list() |> table()

# 4. Volcano plot
let plot_data = results
  |> mutate("neg_log10_p", |row| -log10(row.p_ttest + 1e-300))

volcano(plot_data, "log2fc", "neg_log10_p")
  |> save_plot("complete_volcano.svg")

# 5. Summary
let sig = results
  |> filter_by("p_ttest", |p| p < 0.05)
  |> filter_by("log2fc", |fc| abs(fc) > 1.0)

println("=== Final Summary ===")
println("Total genes tested: " + to_string(len(results)))
println("Significant (p<0.05, |FC|>2): " + to_string(len(sig)))
println("WARNING: p-values are NOT adjusted for multiple testing")
println("See Chapter 8 before reporting these results!")

Where Next?

This chapter covered comparing two groups. But what happens when you have three, four, or five groups? You cannot simply run pairwise t-tests — that creates a multiple testing problem within a single comparison. Chapter 6 covers multi-group comparisons with ANOVA and Kruskal-Wallis.

We also noted repeatedly that testing 20,000 genes requires correction for multiple testing. Chapter 8 covers this critical topic — Bonferroni, Benjamini-Hochberg, and why the choice matters enormously.

And if your data is not normally distributed even after log-transformation, Chapter 7 covers non-parametric methods in detail.

Key Takeaways

  1. Use Welch’s t-test by default, not Student’s
  2. Log-transform expression data before testing
  3. Always compute effect size (Cohen’s d) alongside p-values
  4. Always compute confidence intervals — they tell you more than p-values
  5. Visualize with volcano plots — they combine fold change and significance
  6. Check your assumptions — QQ plots for normality, variance comparison
  7. Use paired tests when samples are naturally paired
  8. Consider Wilcoxon when normality is questionable
  9. Never interpret raw p-values from genome-wide tests (Chapter 8)
  10. Report the complete picture: mean, SD, fold change, CI, p-value, effect size
