Biostatistics in Practice

Correlation & Regression

“Correlation does not imply causation, but it does waggle its eyebrows suggestively and gesture furtively while mouthing ‘look over there.’” — Randall Munroe

The Problem

You have RNA-seq expression data and clinical outcomes for over a thousand breast cancer patients from TCGA. A collaborator asks: “Does ESR1 expression predict survival? What about ERBB2? Can we build a model that uses expression of a handful of genes to predict patient outcomes?”

These are correlation and regression questions. Before you can answer them, you need to understand what correlation actually measures, when it misleads, and how regression lets you disentangle the effects of multiple variables acting simultaneously.

This chapter takes you from pairwise correlation to multiple regression, using real TCGA breast cancer data throughout.

Why It Matters

Clinical Decisions Depend on Associations

Breast cancer treatment has been revolutionized by molecular markers. The estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and HER2 (ERBB2) status determine treatment strategy. These markers were discovered through correlation studies: researchers noticed that expression of certain genes correlated with treatment response and survival.

But not every correlation is a useful biomarker. Thousands of genes correlate with survival in breast cancer — most because they correlate with subtype, which correlates with survival. The challenge is distinguishing direct, useful associations from indirect, confounded ones.

The Stakes of Getting It Wrong

If you report a “survival gene” that is actually correlated with survival only because it is a marker of ER status, you have not found a new biomarker — you have rediscovered ER status with extra steps. Worse, if you build a model that ignores confounders, it will fail when applied to a different cohort.

Regression Gives You Conditional Associations

Simple correlation tells you whether two variables move together. Regression tells you whether they move together after accounting for other variables. This is the tool that lets you ask: “Does gene X predict survival after controlling for age, stage, and subtype?”

The Analysis

Part 1: Pearson Correlation

Pearson correlation (r) measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables. It ranges from -1 (perfect negative linear relationship) to +1 (perfect positive linear relationship). Zero means no linear relationship.

The formula:

r = sum((x_i - mean_x)(y_i - mean_y)) / sqrt(sum((x_i - mean_x)^2) * sum((y_i - mean_y)^2))

Assumptions:

  1. Both variables are continuous
  2. The relationship is linear
  3. Both variables are approximately normally distributed (for inference)
  4. No extreme outliers (they inflate or deflate r dramatically)
# Pearson correlation between ESR1 and PGR
let r_esr1_pgr = cor(esr1_log, pgr_log)
println("Pearson r (ESR1, PGR): " + to_string(round(r_esr1_pgr, 4)))

Expected result: r around 0.65-0.75. ESR1 and PGR are co-regulated — the estrogen receptor drives PGR transcription. This is a biologically meaningful positive correlation.

# ESR1 vs ERBB2
let r_esr1_erbb2 = cor(esr1_log, erbb2_log)
println("Pearson r (ESR1, ERBB2): " + to_string(round(r_esr1_erbb2, 4)))

Expected: r around -0.20 to -0.35. The negative correlation reflects the biological reality that ER+ and HER2+ are largely distinct subtypes, though the correlation is moderate because some tumors are double-positive or double-negative.

# Full correlation matrix for all genes of interest
let gene_names = ["ESR1", "ERBB2", "TP53", "MKI67", "PGR", "BRCA1"]

    col_values(analysis_data, g) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
)

Interpreting the Correlation Matrix

The heatmap reveals the structure of breast cancer biology:

  • ESR1-PGR: Strong positive. Co-regulated by estrogen signaling.
  • ESR1-ERBB2: Negative. Different subtypes.
  • MKI67-ESR1: Negative. High-proliferation tumors tend to be ER-negative.
  • MKI67-ERBB2: Positive. HER2+ tumors tend to be more proliferative.
  • TP53-MKI67: Positive. TP53 mutations associate with high proliferation.
  • BRCA1-ESR1: Negative. BRCA1-related tumors are often triple-negative.

Each of these correlations has a biological explanation. But correlation alone does not tell you which is cause and which is effect. ESR1 drives PGR transcription (causal), but ESR1 and ERBB2 are negatively correlated because they mark different cell lineages (not causal — subtype is the common cause).

Part 2: Correlation Is Not Causation

This point deserves a detailed treatment because genomics is full of misleading correlations.

Example 1: The Subtype Confounder

# Separate ER-positive and ER-negative patients
let er_status = col_values(analysis_data, "breast_carcinoma_estrogen_receptor_status")

let er_pos_idx = er_status |> map(|x| x == "Positive")
let er_neg_idx = er_status |> map(|x| x == "Negative")

# Correlation between MKI67 and ERBB2 in ALL patients
let mki67_all = col_values(analysis_data, "MKI67") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let erbb2_all = col_values(analysis_data, "ERBB2") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let r_all = cor(mki67_all, erbb2_all)
println("MKI67-ERBB2 correlation (all patients): " + to_string(round(r_all, 4)))

# Now separate by ER status
let data_er_pos = analysis_data |> filter_by("breast_carcinoma_estrogen_receptor_status", |x| x == "Positive")
let data_er_neg = analysis_data |> filter_by("breast_carcinoma_estrogen_receptor_status", |x| x == "Negative")

let mki67_pos = col_values(data_er_pos, "MKI67") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let erbb2_pos = col_values(data_er_pos, "ERBB2") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let r_pos = cor(mki67_pos, erbb2_pos)

let mki67_neg = col_values(data_er_neg, "MKI67") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let erbb2_neg = col_values(data_er_neg, "ERBB2") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let r_neg = cor(mki67_neg, erbb2_neg)

println("MKI67-ERBB2 correlation (ER+): " + to_string(round(r_pos, 4)))
println("MKI67-ERBB2 correlation (ER-): " + to_string(round(r_neg, 4)))

This demonstrates a critical phenomenon. The overall correlation between MKI67 and ERBB2 may be positive, but within each ER subgroup, the correlation can be much weaker or even reversed. The overall correlation was driven by ER status acting as a confounder — ER-negative tumors tend to have both higher MKI67 and higher ERBB2 on average, creating an apparent positive correlation.

This is Simpson’s paradox in action: the trend in the combined data does not hold within the subgroups.

Example 2: Batch Effects Create Spurious Correlation

# Suppose two genes are uncorrelated biologically but both are affected
# by library size. Samples processed in batch A have larger libraries.
# Both genes appear "upregulated" in batch A — creating a correlation
# that is purely technical.

# Simulate this to demonstrate the principle
let batch_a_lib = rnorm(50, 100.0, 10.0)  # larger libraries
let batch_b_lib = rnorm(50, 60.0, 10.0)   # smaller libraries
let all_lib = concat(batch_a_lib, batch_b_lib)

# Gene X: no real difference, but expression scales with library size
let gene_x = all_lib |> map(|lib| lib * 0.5 + rnorm(1, 0.0, 5.0) |> head(1))
let gene_x_flat = gene_x |> flatten()

# Gene Y: also no real difference, also scales with library size
let gene_y = all_lib |> map(|lib| lib * 0.3 + rnorm(1, 0.0, 5.0) |> head(1))
let gene_y_flat = gene_y |> flatten()

let r_spurious = cor(gene_x_flat, gene_y_flat)
println("Spurious correlation from batch effect: " + to_string(round(r_spurious, 4)))
# This will be strongly positive despite no biological relationship

Part 3: Spearman Correlation for Non-Linear Relationships

Pearson correlation measures linear relationships. But many biological relationships are monotonic but non-linear: as X increases, Y increases, but not at a constant rate.

Spearman correlation works on ranks rather than raw values. It measures whether the rank order of X matches the rank order of Y.

# Spearman correlation for ESR1 vs PGR
let sp_esr1_pgr = spearman(esr1_log, pgr_log)
println("Spearman rho (ESR1, PGR): " + to_string(round(sp_esr1_pgr.rho, 4)))
println("Spearman p-value: " + to_string(sp_esr1_pgr.p_value))

# Compare Pearson and Spearman for all gene pairs
println("Comparison of Pearson r and Spearman rho:")
println("Gene Pair            Pearson    Spearman")
println("─────────────────    ───────    ────────")

let pairs = [
    ["ESR1", "PGR"], ["ESR1", "ERBB2"], ["ESR1", "MKI67"],
    ["ERBB2", "MKI67"], ["TP53", "MKI67"], ["BRCA1", "ESR1"]
]

pairs |> each(|pair|
    let v1 = col_values(analysis_data, pair[0]) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
    let v2 = col_values(analysis_data, pair[1]) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
    let r = cor(v1, v2)
    let sp = spearman(v1, v2)
    let label = pair[0] + "-" + pair[1]
    println(label + "    " + to_string(round(r, 4)) + "    " + to_string(round(sp.rho, 4)))
)

When Pearson and Spearman agree closely, the relationship is close to linear. When Spearman is notably larger than Pearson, the relationship is monotonic but non-linear — common with expression data where a few extreme values distort the linear fit.

Kendall’s Tau

Kendall’s tau is another rank-based correlation. It counts concordant and discordant pairs. It is more robust than Spearman with small sample sizes and ties.

# Kendall's tau for ESR1 vs PGR
let kt = kendall(esr1_log, pgr_log)
println("Kendall tau (ESR1, PGR): " + to_string(round(kt.tau, 4)))
println("Kendall p-value: " + to_string(kt.p_value))

Kendall’s tau is always smaller in magnitude than Spearman’s rho for the same data. This is normal — they have different scales. The p-values, however, are usually similar.

Part 4: Simple Linear Regression

Correlation tells you that two variables are associated. Regression goes further: it fits a line that predicts one variable from the other and quantifies the prediction.

The model: Y = beta_0 + beta_1 * X + epsilon

  • beta_0: intercept (predicted Y when X = 0)
  • beta_1: slope (change in Y per unit change in X)
  • epsilon: residual error (what the model cannot explain)
# Does ESR1 expression predict overall survival time?
let esr1_expr = col_values(analysis_data, "ESR1") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let survival_days = col_values(analysis_data, "OS.time") |> map(|x| to_float(x))

# Remove any remaining nulls
let valid_idx = zip(esr1_expr, survival_days)
    |> filter(|pair| !is_none(pair[0]) && !is_none(pair[1]))
let esr1_clean = valid_idx |> map(|pair| pair[0])
let surv_clean = valid_idx |> map(|pair| pair[1])

# Fit simple linear regression
let model_simple = lm(surv_clean, esr1_clean)
println("Simple Linear Regression: Survival ~ ESR1")
println("  Intercept (beta_0): " + to_string(round(model_simple.intercept, 2)))
println("  Slope (beta_1): " + to_string(round(model_simple.coefficients[0], 2)))
println("  R-squared: " + to_string(round(model_simple.r_squared, 4)))
println("  P-value (slope): " + to_string(model_simple.p_values[0]))

Interpreting the Simple Regression

The slope (beta_1) tells you: for each unit increase in log2(ESR1 FPKM+1), the predicted survival time changes by beta_1 days. If it is positive, higher ESR1 expression is associated with longer survival; if negative, shorter.

The R-squared tells you what fraction of the variance in survival time is explained by ESR1 expression alone. In genomics, R-squared for a single gene predicting a clinical outcome is usually very small (0.01-0.05). This does not mean the association is unimportant — survival is determined by many factors, and any single gene explains only a small fraction.

The p-value tells you whether the slope is significantly different from zero. With 1,000+ patients, even tiny effects can be significant. This is a case where statistical significance and practical significance diverge.

# Visualize the regression
scatter(esr1_clean, surv_clean, "ESR1 Expression vs Survival Time")
    |> save_plot("esr1_survival_regression.svg")

# QQ plot of residuals to check normality assumption
let residuals = zip(surv_clean, esr1_clean) |> map(|pair|
    pair[0] - (model_simple.intercept + model_simple.coefficients[0] * pair[1])
)
qq_plot(residuals, "Residuals QQ Plot") |> save_plot("esr1_survival_qq.svg")

The QQ plot is crucial. If the residuals are normally distributed, the points fall along the diagonal. Survival data is notoriously non-normal (right-skewed, censored), so the QQ plot will likely show deviation. This means the p-values from linear regression are approximate at best, which is one reason survival analysis (Chapter 11) uses specialized methods.

Part 5: Multiple Regression — Controlling for Confounders

Simple regression of survival on ESR1 ignores everything else about the patient. Older patients have shorter survival regardless of gene expression. Higher-stage tumors have shorter survival. Multiple regression lets us ask: “Does ESR1 predict survival after controlling for age and stage?”

The model: Y = beta_0 + beta_1ESR1 + beta_2Age + beta_3*Stage + epsilon

# Prepare variables for multiple regression
let age_vals = col_values(analysis_data, "age_at_initial_pathologic_diagnosis")
    |> map(|x| to_float(x))

# Encode stage as numeric (simplified)
# Stage I = 1, Stage II = 2, Stage III = 3, Stage IV = 4
let stage_vals = col_values(analysis_data, "pathologic_stage") |> map(|s|
    if contains(to_string(s), "IV") then 4.0
    else if contains(to_string(s), "III") then 3.0
    else if contains(to_string(s), "II") then 2.0
    else if contains(to_string(s), "I") then 1.0
    else 0.0  # unknown/missing
)

# Build predictor matrix: [ESR1, Age, Stage]
let predictors = [esr1_clean, age_vals, stage_vals]
let pred_matrix = matrix(predictors)

# Fit multiple regression
let model_multi = lm(surv_clean, pred_matrix)
println("Multiple Regression: Survival ~ ESR1 + Age + Stage")
println("  Intercept: " + to_string(round(model_multi.intercept, 2)))
println("  ESR1 coefficient: " + to_string(round(model_multi.coefficients[0], 2)))
println("  Age coefficient: " + to_string(round(model_multi.coefficients[1], 2)))
println("  Stage coefficient: " + to_string(round(model_multi.coefficients[2], 2)))
println("  R-squared: " + to_string(round(model_multi.r_squared, 4)))
println("  Adjusted R-squared: " + to_string(round(model_multi.adj_r_squared, 4)))

Interpreting Multiple Regression Coefficients

Each coefficient is now a partial regression coefficient — it represents the effect of that variable holding the other variables constant.

  • ESR1 coefficient: Change in predicted survival per unit increase in log2(ESR1), holding age and stage constant. This is the adjusted effect.
  • Age coefficient: Change in predicted survival per year of age, holding ESR1 and stage constant. Expected to be negative (older patients have shorter survival).
  • Stage coefficient: Change in predicted survival per stage increase, holding ESR1 and age constant. Expected to be strongly negative (higher stage = shorter survival).
# Compare simple vs multiple regression ESR1 coefficient
println("ESR1 effect comparison:")
println("  Unadjusted (simple): " + to_string(round(model_simple.coefficients[0], 2)))
println("  Adjusted (multiple): " + to_string(round(model_multi.coefficients[0], 2)))

If the ESR1 coefficient changes substantially between simple and multiple regression, this tells you that age and stage were confounders. The unadjusted effect was partially attributable to correlations between ESR1, age, and stage.

Part 6: Residual Analysis

The validity of regression depends on its assumptions. Residual analysis checks those assumptions.

Assumptions of linear regression:

  1. Linearity: The relationship between X and Y is linear
  2. Independence: Residuals are independent of each other
  3. Homoscedasticity: Residuals have constant variance
  4. Normality: Residuals are normally distributed
# Calculate residuals from multiple regression
let predicted = zip(esr1_clean, zip(age_vals, stage_vals)) |> map(|pair|
    let esr1_val = pair[0]
    let age_val = pair[1][0]
    let stage_val = pair[1][1]
    model_multi.intercept
        + model_multi.coefficients[0] * esr1_val
        + model_multi.coefficients[1] * age_val
        + model_multi.coefficients[2] * stage_val
)

let resid_multi = zip(surv_clean, predicted) |> map(|pair| pair[0] - pair[1])

# 1. Residuals vs fitted values (check linearity and homoscedasticity)
scatter(predicted, resid_multi, "Residuals vs Fitted Values")
    |> save_plot("residuals_vs_fitted.svg")

# 2. QQ plot of residuals (check normality)
qq_plot(resid_multi, "Multiple Regression Residuals") |> save_plot("multi_reg_qq.svg")

# 3. Residual summary statistics
println("Residual summary:")
println(summary(resid_multi))
println("Residual standard deviation: " + to_string(round(stdev(resid_multi), 2)))

If the residuals-vs-fitted plot shows a funnel shape (wider on one side), you have heteroscedasticity. If it shows a curve, the linearity assumption is violated. For survival data, both problems are common because survival times are bounded below by zero and right-skewed.

Part 7: Gene-Gene Correlation Networks

Beyond pairwise correlations, we can build a network of gene-gene correlations. This reveals modules of co-expressed genes, which often correspond to biological pathways.

# Select top variable genes for correlation network
# First, compute variance across samples for each gene
let expr_data = read_tsv(expr_file)

# Get a subset of known cancer-related genes
let cancer_genes = [
    "ESR1", "ERBB2", "TP53", "MKI67", "PGR", "BRCA1",
    "EGFR", "CDH1", "PTEN", "PIK3CA", "AKT1", "MTOR",
    "MYC", "CCND1", "CDK4", "RB1", "VEGFA", "KRAS",
    "BRAF", "MAP2K1"
]

# Extract expression for these genes across all samples
let cancer_expr = expr_data |> filter_by("Ensembl_ID", |g| contains(cancer_genes, g))

# Build correlation matrix
let cancer_vals = cancer_genes |> map(|g|
    col_values(analysis_data, g) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
)
let cancer_matrix = matrix(cancer_vals)
let cancer_cor = cor_matrix(cancer_matrix)

# Heatmap of the correlation network
heatmap(cancer_cor, cancer_genes, cancer_genes, "Cancer Gene Correlation Network")
    |> save_plot("cancer_gene_network.svg")

# Build a graph where edges represent strong correlations
let gene_network = graph()

# Add nodes
cancer_genes |> each(|g| add_node(gene_network, g))

# Add edges for correlations above threshold
let threshold = 0.3
let n_genes = len(cancer_genes)

let edge_count = 0
let i = 0
cancer_genes |> each(|g1|
    let j = i + 1
    slice(cancer_genes, j, n_genes) |> each(|g2|
        let v1 = col_values(analysis_data, g1) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
        let v2 = col_values(analysis_data, g2) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
        let r_val = abs(cor(v1, v2))
        if r_val > threshold then
            add_edge(gene_network, g1, g2)
            edge_count = edge_count + 1
        end
    )
    i = i + 1
)

println("Gene correlation network:")
println("  Nodes: " + to_string(len(cancer_genes)))
println("  Edges (|r| > " + to_string(threshold) + "): " + to_string(edge_count))

# Find connected components — these are co-expression modules
let components = connected_components(gene_network)
println("  Connected components: " + to_string(len(components)))

# Show the degree of each gene (number of strong correlations)
cancer_genes |> each(|g|
    let deg = degree(gene_network, g)
    println("  " + g + ": " + to_string(deg) + " strong correlations")
)

Genes with high degree are “hub” genes — they correlate with many other genes. In cancer biology, hub genes often correspond to transcription factors or signaling pathway components that regulate many downstream targets.

Part 8: Regression to the Mean

Regression to the mean is one of the most misunderstood phenomena in biostatistics. It was discovered by Francis Galton (who also coined the term “regression” — the method is named after this phenomenon).

The trap: If you select patients with the highest gene expression at time point 1 and measure them again at time point 2, their expression will appear to decrease — even if nothing has changed. This is because extreme values include measurement error, and that error is unlikely to be equally extreme at the next measurement.

# Demonstrate regression to the mean with simulated data
# True expression is constant, but measurement adds noise

let n_patients = 200
let true_expr = rnorm(n_patients, 10.0, 2.0)  # true expression

# Measurement 1: true + noise
let noise1 = rnorm(n_patients, 0.0, 1.5)
let measure1 = zip(true_expr, noise1) |> map(|pair| pair[0] + pair[1])

# Measurement 2: true + different noise
let noise2 = rnorm(n_patients, 0.0, 1.5)
let measure2 = zip(true_expr, noise2) |> map(|pair| pair[0] + pair[1])

# Select the top 20% from measurement 1
let sorted_m1 = measure1 |> sort() |> to_list()
let cutoff = quantile(measure1, 0.80)
let top_idx = measure1 |> map(|x| x >= cutoff)

let top_m1 = zip(measure1, top_idx) |> filter(|pair| pair[1]) |> map(|pair| pair[0])
let top_m2 = zip(measure2, top_idx) |> filter(|pair| pair[1]) |> map(|pair| pair[0])

println("Regression to the mean demonstration:")
println("  Mean of top 20% at time 1: " + to_string(round(mean(top_m1), 2)))
println("  Mean of same patients at time 2: " + to_string(round(mean(top_m2), 2)))
println("  Apparent decrease: " + to_string(round(mean(top_m1) - mean(top_m2), 2)))
println("  (This 'decrease' is purely statistical, not biological)")

This is devastating for pre-post studies. If you select patients with the highest tumor marker levels and give them a treatment, the marker will likely decrease at follow-up even if the treatment does nothing. Regression to the mean masquerades as a treatment effect.

The solution: Use a control group. If both treatment and control groups are selected for high values, both will regress to the mean. Only the difference between groups reflects a true treatment effect.

# Show the same phenomenon with a scatter plot
scatter(measure1, measure2, "Measurement 1 vs Measurement 2")
    |> save_plot("regression_to_mean.svg")

# The slope of the regression line is less than 1.0
let rtm_model = lm(measure2, measure1)
println("Regression slope (should be < 1 if regression to mean): "
    + to_string(round(rtm_model.coefficients[0], 4)))

A slope less than 1.0 means that extreme values on X are associated with less extreme values on Y. This is regression to the mean. The more measurement noise, the stronger the effect.

Part 9: Building a Practical Prediction Model

Now let us combine everything into a practical prediction model for breast cancer outcomes.

# Build a multi-gene model predicting survival
# Include: ESR1, ERBB2, MKI67, Age, Stage

let esr1_v = col_values(analysis_data, "ESR1") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let erbb2_v = col_values(analysis_data, "ERBB2") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let mki67_v = col_values(analysis_data, "MKI67") |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
let age_v = col_values(analysis_data, "age_at_initial_pathologic_diagnosis")
    |> map(|x| to_float(x))
let stage_v = col_values(analysis_data, "pathologic_stage") |> map(|s|
    if contains(to_string(s), "IV") then 4.0
    else if contains(to_string(s), "III") then 3.0
    else if contains(to_string(s), "II") then 2.0
    else if contains(to_string(s), "I") then 1.0
    else 0.0
)
let surv_v = col_values(analysis_data, "OS.time") |> map(|x| to_float(x))

# Fit the full model
let pred_full = matrix([esr1_v, erbb2_v, mki67_v, age_v, stage_v])
let model_full = lm(surv_v, pred_full)

println("Full Model: Survival ~ ESR1 + ERBB2 + MKI67 + Age + Stage")
println("  R-squared: " + to_string(round(model_full.r_squared, 4)))
println("  Adjusted R-squared: " + to_string(round(model_full.adj_r_squared, 4)))
println("")
println("  Coefficients:")
let coef_names = ["ESR1", "ERBB2", "MKI67", "Age", "Stage"]
zip(coef_names, model_full.coefficients) |> each(|pair|
    println("    " + pair[0] + ": " + to_string(round(pair[1], 4))
        + "  (p = " + to_string(round(model_full.p_values[0], 4)) + ")")
)

# Compare models: simple vs multiple
println("Model comparison:")
println("  ESR1 only:        R^2 = " + to_string(round(model_simple.r_squared, 4)))
println("  ESR1+Age+Stage:   R^2 = " + to_string(round(model_multi.r_squared, 4)))
println("  Full (5 vars):    R^2 = " + to_string(round(model_full.r_squared, 4)))

The improvement from adding more variables shows you how much additional variance each variable explains. But beware: adding more variables always increases R-squared (even random variables will increase it). The adjusted R-squared penalizes for the number of predictors and is a better guide.

# Normalize coefficients to compare effect sizes
# Standardize each predictor to mean 0, SD 1
let esr1_z = normalize(esr1_v)
let erbb2_z = normalize(erbb2_v)
let mki67_z = normalize(mki67_v)
let age_z = normalize(age_v)
let stage_z = normalize(stage_v)

let pred_std = matrix([esr1_z, erbb2_z, mki67_z, age_z, stage_z])
let model_std = lm(surv_v, pred_std)

println("Standardized coefficients (effect size comparison):")
zip(coef_names, model_std.coefficients) |> each(|pair|
    println("  " + pair[0] + ": " + to_string(round(pair[1], 2)))
)

Standardized coefficients are directly comparable: the variable with the largest absolute standardized coefficient has the strongest effect on survival. Typically, stage dominates, followed by age, with individual gene effects being smaller but still meaningful.

Interpretation

What We Found

  1. ESR1 and PGR are strongly positively correlated (r ~ 0.7), reflecting the biology of estrogen receptor signaling.

  2. ESR1 and ERBB2 are negatively correlated (r ~ -0.25), reflecting distinct breast cancer subtypes.

  3. ESR1 expression predicts survival in simple regression, but the effect is partially confounded by age and stage.

  4. Multiple regression reveals the adjusted effect: after controlling for clinical variables, the independent contribution of ESR1 to survival prediction is smaller than the unadjusted effect suggests.

  5. Stage is the dominant predictor of survival time, as expected.

  6. Gene-gene correlation networks reveal modules of co-expressed genes corresponding to known biological pathways.

What We Cannot Conclude

  • We cannot say ESR1 expression causes longer survival. ER-positive tumors are a distinct subtype with different biology, different treatment (tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors), and different prognosis.

  • We cannot use this linear regression model for clinical prediction of survival. Survival data requires specialized methods (Chapter 11) because of censoring.

  • We cannot conclude that low-correlation genes are biologically unrelated. Correlation measures linear (or rank-based) association. Non-linear relationships, threshold effects, and interactions are invisible to correlation.

Decision

When to Report a Correlation

Report correlations when:

  • The sample size is large enough for the confidence interval to be narrow
  • You have checked that the relationship is not driven by outliers
  • You have considered and addressed potential confounders
  • You report the confidence interval, not just the point estimate

When to Use Multiple Regression

Use multiple regression when:

  • You want the effect of X on Y adjusting for other variables
  • You suspect confounders (age, batch, clinical variables)
  • You want to compare the relative importance of multiple predictors
  • The outcome variable is continuous and approximately normal

When to Use Something Else

  • Binary outcome (alive/dead, mutated/wild-type): Use logistic regression (Chapter 10)
  • Survival time with censoring: Use Cox regression (Chapter 11)
  • Count data (read counts, variant counts): Use Poisson or negative binomial regression (Chapter 10)
  • Non-linear relationships: Consider spline regression or generalized additive models

Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Ignoring Non-Linearity

# Pearson correlation can be zero even with a strong non-linear relationship
# Example: quadratic relationship
let x_vals = rnorm(200, 0.0, 3.0)
let y_quad = x_vals |> map(|x| pow(x, 2.0) + rnorm(1, 0.0, 1.0) |> head(1))
let y_flat = y_quad |> flatten()

let r_quad = cor(x_vals, y_flat)
println("Pearson r for quadratic relationship: " + to_string(round(r_quad, 4)))
println("(Near zero despite perfect quadratic relationship)")

# Always plot your data!
scatter(x_vals, y_flat, "Quadratic Relationship (r ~ 0)")
    |> save_plot("quadratic_relationship.svg")

Pitfall 2: Correlation of Ratios

In genomics, we often correlate ratios or normalized values. This creates spurious correlations because ratios share denominators.

If X = A/C and Y = B/C, then X and Y will be correlated even if A and B are independent — because both depend on C. This affects:

  • FPKM values (normalized by library size)
  • Compositional data (proportions that sum to 1)
  • Fold changes relative to the same reference
# Demonstrate spurious ratio correlation
let a_vals = rnorm(100, 50.0, 10.0)  # independent
let b_vals = rnorm(100, 50.0, 10.0)  # independent
let c_vals = rnorm(100, 50.0, 10.0)  # shared denominator

let ratio_x = zip(a_vals, c_vals) |> map(|pair| pair[0] / pair[1])
let ratio_y = zip(b_vals, c_vals) |> map(|pair| pair[0] / pair[1])

let r_ratio = cor(ratio_x, ratio_y)
println("Correlation of A/C and B/C (A, B independent): " + to_string(round(r_ratio, 4)))
println("(Positive despite A and B being independent)")

Pitfall 3: Extrapolation

Linear regression fits a line through the observed range of data. Predicting outside that range (extrapolation) is unreliable because the relationship may not be linear beyond the data.

If your TCGA patients range from age 25 to 90, do not use the model to predict survival for a 15-year-old. The linear relationship between age and survival may not hold at extreme ages.

Pitfall 4: Multicollinearity

When predictors in multiple regression are highly correlated with each other, the individual coefficients become unstable. This is multicollinearity.

# ESR1 and PGR are highly correlated — including both may cause instability
let pred_collinear = matrix([esr1_v, pgr_log, age_v])
let model_collinear = lm(surv_v, pred_collinear)

println("Model with collinear predictors (ESR1 + PGR + Age):")
println("  ESR1 coefficient: " + to_string(round(model_collinear.coefficients[0], 4)))
println("  PGR coefficient: " + to_string(round(model_collinear.coefficients[1], 4)))
println("  R-squared: " + to_string(round(model_collinear.r_squared, 4)))

# Compare to models with only one of the two
let model_esr1_only = lm(surv_v, matrix([esr1_v, age_v]))
let model_pgr_only = lm(surv_v, matrix([pgr_log, age_v]))

println("ESR1 + Age only R^2: " + to_string(round(model_esr1_only.r_squared, 4)))
println("PGR + Age only R^2: " + to_string(round(model_pgr_only.r_squared, 4)))

When two predictors carry largely the same information, adding both does not improve prediction and makes individual coefficients unreliable. Choose one or use dimensionality reduction.

Pitfall 5: Overfitting

With enough predictors, you can fit any dataset perfectly. But the model will not generalize to new data.

Rule of thumb: You need at least 10-15 observations per predictor variable. With 1,000 patients, a model with 5 predictors is fine. A model with 200 gene predictors needs 2,000-3,000 patients.

# Demonstrate with adjusted R-squared
println("Adjusted R-squared penalizes for number of predictors:")
println("  Simple (1 predictor): adj R^2 = " + to_string(round(model_simple.adj_r_squared, 4)))
println("  Multiple (3 predictors): adj R^2 = " + to_string(round(model_multi.adj_r_squared, 4)))
println("  Full (5 predictors): adj R^2 = " + to_string(round(model_full.adj_r_squared, 4)))
println("If adj R^2 stops increasing or decreases, added variables are not helping.")

Pitfall 6: P-value Fishing

With 20,000 genes, testing each one for correlation with survival will produce ~1,000 “significant” results at p < 0.05 by chance alone. Always correct for multiple testing when scanning many genes.

# Suppose we tested 100 genes for correlation with survival
# Collect p-values and apply correction
let test_genes = slice(cancer_genes, 0, 10)  # using our subset for demonstration

let p_values_raw = test_genes |> map(|g|
    let gv = col_values(analysis_data, g) |> map(|x| log2(to_float(x) + 1.0))
    let sp = spearman(gv, surv_v)
    sp.p_value
)

let p_adj = p_adjust(p_values_raw, "BH")

println("Gene          Raw P         BH-adjusted P")
println("────────      ─────         ─────────────")
zip(test_genes, zip(p_values_raw, p_adj)) |> each(|pair|
    println(pair[0] + "    " + to_string(round(pair[1][0], 6))
        + "    " + to_string(round(pair[1][1], 6)))
)

Where Next?

Immediate Extensions

  • Logistic regression: When the outcome is binary (Chapter 10). Use GLM with logit link to predict ER status from gene expression.

  • Cox regression: When the outcome is survival time with censoring (Chapter 11). The correct approach for the survival question we explored here with imperfect linear regression.

  • Partial correlation: Correlation between X and Y after removing the effect of Z. Computable by correlating the residuals from regressing X on Z and Y on Z.

Advanced Topics

  • Ridge and Lasso regression: When you have more predictors than samples (the genomics default). Regularization prevents overfitting.

  • Principal component regression: Use PCA to reduce thousands of genes to a handful of components, then regress survival on the components.

  • Weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA): Scale gene-gene correlation networks to the entire transcriptome. Identify modules and relate them to clinical traits.

Practice Problems

  1. Hormone receptor genes: Compute the correlation matrix for all known hormone receptor genes (ESR1, ESR2, PGR, AR) and their downstream targets in the TCGA BRCA dataset. Which pairs have the strongest correlations?

  2. Confounding by subtype: Run simple regression of MKI67 on ERBB2 in all patients, then separately within PAM50 subtypes. Does the relationship change? This demonstrates that subtype acts as a confounder.

  3. Effect of log transformation: Compare Pearson correlations between ESR1 and PGR on raw FPKM values vs log2-transformed values. How much does the transformation change the result? Plot both.

  4. Regression to the mean: Using two replicate measurements from the same samples (if available), demonstrate regression to the mean by selecting extreme samples from replicate 1 and measuring their values in replicate 2.

  5. Multicollinearity: Build a regression model using 10 genes from the same pathway (e.g., the PI3K-AKT pathway). Examine how unstable the individual coefficients are. Then use PCA on the 10 genes and regress survival on the first 2 principal components instead.

Key takeaways from this chapter:

  • Correlation measures association, not causation. Always consider confounders.
  • Always visualize the data before computing correlations — scatter plots reveal non-linearity, outliers, and subgroups that numbers hide.
  • Multiple regression adjusts for confounders but assumes linearity and independence.
  • Spearman and Kendall correlations handle non-linear monotonic relationships.
  • Regression to the mean traps pre-post studies without control groups.
  • Multicollinearity makes individual coefficients in multiple regression unreliable when predictors are correlated.
  • Gene-gene correlation networks reveal co-expression modules, but edges are not causal relationships.
  • With thousands of genes, always correct for multiple testing.
